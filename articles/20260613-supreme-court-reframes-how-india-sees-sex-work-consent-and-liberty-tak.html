Supreme Court reframes how India sees sex work: consent and liberty take centre stage

What happened

The Times of India reports that the Supreme Court has issued a significant ruling that reframes the legal understanding of sex work in India, centering the concepts of consent and liberty rather than moral judgment or blanket criminality. The court's pronouncement marks a shift from treating all sex work as inherently criminal or immoral, instead recognising the agency of consenting adults. The ruling is expected to have wide implications for how law enforcement interacts with sex workers, and how lower courts adjudicate related cases. It builds on earlier Supreme Court directions that had asked state authorities to treat sex workers with dignity and not arrest consenting adults. This latest judgment is seen as one of the most progressive stances an Indian court has taken on the issue.

The context

Sex work in India occupies a complex legal grey zone — it is not explicitly illegal, but activities surrounding it, such as running a brothel or soliciting, are criminalised under the Immoral Traffic (Prevention) Act of 1956. For decades, sex workers have faced routine police harassment, abuse, and denial of basic rights including healthcare and legal recourse. Activist groups and organisations like the Durbar Mahila Samanwaya Committee have long lobbied for decriminalisation and the recognition of sex work as labour. The Supreme Court had in 2022 directed police not to harass or arrest consenting adult sex workers, a precursor to this more expansive ruling. India has an estimated several hundred thousand to over a million sex workers, many of whom live in conditions of extreme vulnerability.

Why it matters

For sex workers across India, this ruling could translate into tangible protection from arbitrary police action and greater access to healthcare, legal aid, and social services. It signals a broader judicial willingness to prioritise individual liberty and consent over moralistic state intervention in private life. Lower courts and police forces will now need to recalibrate their approach, potentially reducing the abuse and extortion that sex workers routinely face. The judgment also has implications for ongoing legislative debates about reforming the Immoral Traffic (Prevention) Act.

What's next

Expect rights organisations to use this judgment to push for legislative reform and to challenge ongoing prosecutions of sex workers in lower courts. The central government and state governments are likely to respond with revised policing guidelines, though implementation will be closely watched by civil society groups.
