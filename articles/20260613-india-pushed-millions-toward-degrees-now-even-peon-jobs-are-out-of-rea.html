India pushed millions toward degrees — now even peon jobs are out of reach

What happened

India Today reports on a growing crisis of graduate unemployment in India, where the expansion of degree programmes has vastly outpaced the creation of suitable jobs. The report notes that degree-holders are now competing even for low-wage, low-skill government positions such as peon roles, which has become a widely observed phenomenon in recruitment drives across states. The credential inflation means that having a degree no longer guarantees employment at a level commensurate with the education received. The story draws attention to a structural mismatch between what India's educational institutions produce and what the economy demands.

The context

India has seen a massive expansion of higher education institutions over the past two decades, with the number of colleges and universities growing substantially and enrolment rising sharply. However, job creation, particularly in the formal sector, has not kept pace with the supply of graduates. Government data and independent research have repeatedly shown high rates of unemployment and underemployment among educated youth. The phenomenon of overqualified candidates applying for menial government jobs — such as instances where thousands of engineering and postgraduate degree-holders applied for posts requiring Class 10 qualifications — has been widely reported. Structural issues including a weak manufacturing base, limited formal private sector job creation, and a mismatch between curriculum and industry needs have been identified as root causes.

Why it matters

For the millions of young Indians who invested time and money in college degrees expecting better life outcomes, this crisis represents a broken social contract. Families, particularly from middle and lower-income backgrounds, often take on significant debt or sacrifice to fund higher education, making the failure to find commensurate employment a financial as well as a psychological blow. The issue has broader economic implications: an underemployed, frustrated educated workforce is a drag on productivity, consumption, and social stability. It also calls into question the return on India's massive public and private investment in higher education infrastructure.

What's next

Expect renewed debate in policy circles and Parliament about the quality and relevance of India's higher education system and its alignment with labour market needs. The government may face pressure to accelerate vocational training and skill development programmes as an alternative pathway. Employment data ahead of state elections will keep this issue politically sensitive.
