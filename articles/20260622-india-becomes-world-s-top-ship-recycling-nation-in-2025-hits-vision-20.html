India becomes world's top ship recycling nation in 2025, hits Vision 2030 target five years early

Key facts

  • India ranked world's No.1 ship recycling nation in 2025
  • Vision 2030 target achieved five years ahead of schedule
  • Alang, Gujarat is the world's largest ship-breaking facility
  • Surpasses Bangladesh, Pakistan, and Turkey in tonnage

India has claimed the top spot globally in ship recycling in 2025, achieving its Vision 2030 target five years ahead of the planned deadline, in a landmark moment for the country's maritime sector. The milestone, reported by Mint, cements India's position above traditional rivals Bangladesh, Pakistan, and Turkey in the volume of ships broken down and processed annually.

The bulk of this achievement is driven by the Alang ship-breaking yard in Gujarat's Bhavnagar district, which is already the world's largest such facility by area. Alang processes hundreds of vessels each year, recovering steel, machinery, and other recyclable materials that feed into India's secondary steel market. The yard employs tens of thousands of workers directly and supports a large ancillary economy of traders and recyclers.

India's rise to the top has been supported by regulatory improvements, including the adoption of the Hong Kong Convention standards for safe and environmentally sound ship recycling, which has made Indian yards more attractive to European and other shipowners who are under increasing pressure to use compliant facilities. The government's push to formalise the sector and improve safety standards has helped differentiate Indian yards from lower-cost but less-regulated competitors.

The early achievement of the Vision 2030 goal is a significant policy win for the shipping and ports ministry, and it positions India as a key player in the global transition toward more sustainable maritime practices. With global shipping fleets continuing to age and a wave of older vessels due for retirement, India's leadership in this sector is likely to generate growing economic returns.
