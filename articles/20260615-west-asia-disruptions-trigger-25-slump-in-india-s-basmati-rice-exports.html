West Asia disruptions trigger 25% slump in India's basmati rice exports; Gulf holds 40-lakh tonne share of India's 60 lakh tonne basmati trade

What happened

The Indian Express reports that West Asia disruptions have caused a 25% slump in India's basmati rice exports, with the Gulf region accounting for 40 lakh tonnes of India's 60 lakh tonne annual basmati trade. The disruption has hurt export revenues and farmer realisations at a time when India depends heavily on Gulf demand for this premium crop.

The context

India is the world's dominant basmati rice exporter, and the Gulf states — particularly Saudi Arabia, UAE, Iraq, and Iran — are its largest buyers. Basmati exports are a major source of agricultural foreign exchange and directly support millions of farmers in northwestern India. Any geopolitical tension in West Asia quickly ripples through to farm gate prices.

Why it matters

A sustained 25% export slump reduces foreign exchange earnings and depresses prices for basmati farmers, who are already navigating input cost pressures. A resolution of West Asian tensions and the reopening of Hormuz could reverse this trend and restore a key revenue stream.

What's next

Expect Indian exporters and the Agricultural and Processed Food Products Export Development Authority (APEDA) to work to rebuild Gulf order books quickly if the Iran-US deal holds and shipping normalises.
