The Challenge of India's Digital Sovereignty: Can the Country Control Its Own Data Future?

Key facts

  • India's digital infrastructure remains heavily dependent on foreign platforms and cloud providers
  • Digital Personal Data Protection Act is a key recent policy step
  • UPI and DigiLocker cited as successful homegrown digital platforms
  • Semiconductor self-sufficiency remains a major unresolved gap

India's ambitions to become a global technology power sit in uncomfortable tension with a foundational vulnerability: the country's digital infrastructure remains deeply dependent on foreign platforms, cloud providers, and semiconductor supply chains. The Hindu's analysis of India's digital sovereignty challenge lays out the structural difficulties of changing that equation without sacrificing the openness and connectivity that have powered the country's digital economy.

Digital sovereignty broadly refers to a nation's ability to control its own data, digital infrastructure, and technology governance. For India, this means everything from where citizen data is stored and who can access it, to which companies build the AI systems that influence Indian users, to whether India has domestic alternatives to dominant foreign platforms in critical sectors like payments, communications, and cloud computing.

India has made meaningful moves in this direction — the Digital Personal Data Protection Act, the push for domestic semiconductor fabrication, the success of homegrown platforms like UPI and DigiLocker, and restrictions on certain foreign applications. But critics argue these steps remain fragmented and that India lacks the deep industrial base in hardware and foundational AI to be genuinely sovereign in a technical sense.

The debate is not merely academic. Geopolitical tensions, as demonstrated by India's past actions against Chinese apps, have shown that digital dependence can become a national security issue overnight. The concentration of cloud and AI infrastructure in American hands poses different but equally real questions about data access, pricing power, and governance.

For India's policymakers, technologists, and businesses, the digital sovereignty question will define the country's strategic posture in technology for the next decade — and the choices made now will be difficult to reverse.
