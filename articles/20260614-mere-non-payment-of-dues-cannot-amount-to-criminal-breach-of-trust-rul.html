Mere non-payment of dues cannot amount to criminal breach of trust, rules Supreme Court

What happened

Deccan Herald reports that the Supreme Court has held that mere non-payment of dues cannot be treated as criminal breach of trust. The court drew a distinction between a failure to fulfil a financial obligation and the intent required to establish a criminal offence under the Indian Penal Code. The ruling is likely to have a significant bearing on the large number of cases where criminal breach of trust charges are filed over essentially civil payment disputes.

The context

Criminal breach of trust under Section 405 of the IPC (now mirrored in the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita) requires proof of entrustment and dishonest misappropriation — not merely a failure to pay. However, the provision has frequently been misused in India to file criminal cases in what are essentially civil or commercial disputes, as a coercive tactic to force settlement. Courts have periodically tried to curb this misuse, and this ruling adds further judicial weight to those efforts.

Why it matters

Businesses, contractors and individuals facing criminal cases over payment disputes can now invoke this ruling to seek quashing of charges, reducing the misuse of criminal law as a commercial pressure tool. It also eases the burden on criminal courts already overwhelmed with cases.

What's next

Expect lawyers and litigants to cite this judgment in High Courts seeking to quash criminal breach of trust FIRs filed over civil payment disputes.
