Strait of Hormuz reopening to ease oil supply risks for India

What happened

The Hindu reports that the reopening of the Strait of Hormuz following the Iran-US accord is expected to ease oil supply risks for India. The strait had been a point of concern during the West Asian tensions, threatening crude shipments to major importers. India stands to benefit significantly given its heavy dependence on Gulf oil.

The context

The Strait of Hormuz is the narrow waterway between Iran and Oman through which roughly 20% of the world's oil supply transits daily. Any threat to its operation sends crude prices higher globally. India, as the world's third-largest oil importer, is acutely vulnerable to Hormuz disruptions, which raise both fuel prices and the current account deficit.

Why it matters

Lower crude import costs would ease India's current account deficit, reduce inflationary pressure, and could eventually translate into stable or lower retail fuel prices for consumers. It also gives the government more fiscal headroom ahead of an uncertain monsoon season.

What's next

Expect oil markets to price in the Hormuz reopening over the next few trading sessions, with Brent crude likely moving lower if the peace deal holds.
