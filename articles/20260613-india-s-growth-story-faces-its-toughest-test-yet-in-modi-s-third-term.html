India's Growth Story Faces Its Toughest Test Yet in Modi's Third Term

What happened

CNBC reports that India's growth story is facing its most challenging period during Modi's third term as Prime Minister. The assessment frames the current juncture as particularly consequential, with multiple economic pressures — including weaker investment, consumption constraints, and global trade headwinds — testing the country's growth resilience simultaneously. The report reflects a broader international concern about whether India can sustain the growth trajectory that has made it a global economic centrepiece in recent years. No specific GDP figures are cited in the headline, but the framing aligns with projections from other analysts pointing to a moderation in growth.

The context

Modi's third term, which began after the 2024 general election, has inherited an economy that, while strong by global standards, faces real structural challenges. Private sector investment has remained below potential despite government incentives, and consumption growth has been uneven across income groups. The government's Production Linked Incentive schemes and infrastructure push are long-term bets that have yet to fully materialise in employment and output numbers. Meanwhile, the global environment — including US tariffs, a strong dollar, and geopolitical uncertainty — has complicated India's export ambitions. India's demographic dividend remains its biggest long-term asset, but capitalising on it requires sustained high growth.

Why it matters

If India's growth stumbles during this critical window, the consequences cascade: fewer formal jobs for young entrants, slower poverty reduction, and a government under fiscal pressure to deliver on both welfare and infrastructure promises simultaneously. India's ambition to become a developed nation by 2047 — a stated Modi government goal — depends on compounding high growth rates without interruption through this decade.

What's next

Expect heightened scrutiny of quarterly GDP data and the Union Budget's fiscal strategy as bellwethers for whether the government can engineer a growth revival. Global rating agencies and multilateral lenders will likely revise their India forecasts in coming months, which will influence foreign investment sentiment.
