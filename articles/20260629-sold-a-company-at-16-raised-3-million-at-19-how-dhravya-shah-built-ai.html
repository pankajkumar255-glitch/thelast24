Sold a company at 16, raised $3 million at 19: How Dhravya Shah built AI startup Supermemory

Key facts

  • Dhravya Shah sold a company at age 16
  • Raised $3 million at age 19
  • Received US O-1 visa for extraordinary ability
  • Founded AI startup Supermemory

Dhravya Shah has done what most entrepreneurs spend decades trying to achieve — and he did it before turning 20. The Indian-origin founder sold a company at 16, raised $3 million at 19, and earned a US O-1 visa, a category reserved for individuals who demonstrate extraordinary ability in their field. His current venture is Supermemory, an AI startup that has drawn significant attention in global tech circles.

Shah's trajectory reflects a broader shift in how young Indians are engaging with technology entrepreneurship. Where earlier generations often pursued traditional engineering or management careers before attempting a startup, a new cohort is building and selling companies in their mid-teens, leveraging open-source tools, global online communities, and remote-first investor networks to compress the startup timeline dramatically.

The O-1 visa recognition is particularly noteworthy. The US immigration category is notoriously difficult to obtain, typically requiring evidence of sustained national or international acclaim — making Shah's receipt of it at 19 an unusual achievement that underscores how seriously American institutions are taking young global tech talent.

Shah's story has gone viral in Indian tech communities, reigniting debate about whether India's education system adequately nurtures or, alternatively, inadvertently suppresses this kind of unconventional ambition. For every Dhravya Shah who breaks through, critics argue, there are thousands of equally capable young Indians whose entrepreneurial instincts are never given room to develop. His success, reported by The Times of India, is as much a conversation-starter about India's talent ecosystem as it is a personal achievement.
