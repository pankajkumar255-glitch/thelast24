Mere non-payment of dues is not criminal breach of trust, rules Supreme Court

What happened

Deccan Herald reports that the Supreme Court has ruled that the mere non-payment of dues cannot amount to criminal breach of trust, which is an offence under Section 405 and related provisions of the Indian Penal Code. The court appears to have been hearing an appeal where criminal breach of trust charges had been filed over a failure to pay dues, and it found that such a scenario does not satisfy the legal ingredients of the offence. The judgment distinguishes between a failure to pay — which is a civil matter — and dishonest misappropriation or conversion of entrusted property, which constitutes the actual criminal offence.

The context

Criminal breach of trust under the IPC requires proof that property was entrusted to a person, and that person dishonestly misappropriated or converted it for their own use. Indian courts have long noted the tendency of parties to file criminal complaints — particularly under Section 406 (punishment for criminal breach of trust) and Section 420 (cheating) — to gain leverage in what are fundamentally civil or commercial disputes. The Supreme Court and various High Courts have repeatedly tried to curb this practice, emphasising that civil remedies should be the primary recourse for non-payment of dues. The ruling fits into a broader judicial trend of protecting individuals and businesses from the criminalisation of contractual failures.

Why it matters

This judgment could reduce the number of frivolous criminal cases filed over commercial disputes, easing the burden on an already overcrowded criminal justice system. Businesses, especially small and medium enterprises, often face criminal harassment when payment disputes arise; this ruling strengthens their legal position. It also reinforces that creditors must pursue civil remedies — such as recovery suits or insolvency proceedings — rather than using criminal law as a pressure tool. Courts and police will now have clearer guidance to reject or quash criminal complaints that are merely dressed-up payment disputes.

What's next

Expect defence lawyers to cite this ruling widely in seeking dismissal or quashing of pending criminal breach of trust cases rooted in non-payment disputes. High Courts across India are likely to follow this precedent in filtering out cases where the facts disclose no dishonest entrustment or misappropriation.
