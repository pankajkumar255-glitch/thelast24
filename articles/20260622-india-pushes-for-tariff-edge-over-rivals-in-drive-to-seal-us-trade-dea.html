India pushes for tariff edge over rivals in drive to seal US trade deal

Key facts

  • India wants lower US tariffs than rivals like China and Vietnam
  • Trade Minister Piyush Goyal confirmed preferential access is the ask
  • Negotiations aim to finalise a bilateral India-US trade deal
  • US reciprocal tariff pause gives India a narrow window to secure terms

India is pushing Washington for a tariff advantage over rival exporting nations as it races to finalise a bilateral trade deal with the United States, with Trade Minister Piyush Goyal publicly signalling that preferential market access is a core Indian demand in the ongoing negotiations.

The ask is strategically pointed: New Delhi wants Indian goods — particularly in sectors like textiles, pharmaceuticals, engineering goods, and electronics — to enter the US market at lower duties than those faced by competitors such as China, Vietnam, and Bangladesh. Such an edge could be decisive in redirecting global supply chains toward India at a moment when US-China trade tensions remain high and American companies are actively seeking to diversify sourcing.

The push comes as multiple countries race to strike their own trade arrangements with Washington following President Donald Trump's sweeping tariff actions earlier this year. India, which currently faces a reciprocal tariff pause until July, is keen to convert that window into a durable deal rather than a temporary reprieve. Negotiations have been ongoing, but the two sides have yet to bridge gaps on agricultural market access, digital trade rules, and intellectual property.

For India, the strategic logic is clear: a binding tariff preference in the world's largest consumer market would accelerate the government's push to build India into a global manufacturing hub, create export-linked employment, and attract foreign investment. The coming weeks of negotiation will test whether Washington is willing to extend the kind of asymmetric advantage New Delhi is seeking.
