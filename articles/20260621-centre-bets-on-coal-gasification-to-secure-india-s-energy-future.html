Centre Bets on Coal Gasification to Secure India's Energy Future

Key facts

  • Centre prioritising coal gasification to cut oil and gas import dependence
  • Syngas from coal can fuel power, industry, fertiliser and methanol production
  • India holds large domestic coal reserves but imports most of its crude oil
  • Coal India and GAIL among state enterprises piloting gasification projects

The Central government is making coal gasification a cornerstone of its energy security strategy, positioning the technology as a way to reduce India's costly dependence on imported oil and gas by putting the country's enormous domestic coal reserves to more productive use. The policy push was outlined in comments reported by Business Standard, with officials framing coal gasification not merely as an industrial technology but as a strategic necessity for a country that remains heavily exposed to global fossil fuel price volatility.

Coal gasification works by converting solid coal into syngas — a mixture of hydrogen and carbon monoxide — which can power electricity generation, substitute for natural gas in industrial processes, or serve as a chemical feedstock for producing fertilisers, methanol, and other critical inputs. For India, which sits on some of the world's largest coal reserves but imports the majority of its crude oil and liquefied natural gas, the logic is straightforward: use what you have rather than buying what you lack.

The government has been trying to push coal gasification for several years, with Coal India and GAIL among the state-owned enterprises tasked with piloting projects. Progress has been slower than hoped, with technology costs, water consumption concerns, and competition from rapidly falling renewable energy prices all complicating the case. The renewed emphasis on energy security — sharpened by the global energy disruptions of recent years — appears to be giving the policy fresh urgency.

Critics note that coal gasification, while reducing import dependency, still produces significant carbon emissions and risks locking India into fossil fuel infrastructure at a time when the world is decarbonising. The government's challenge is to balance short-term energy security with its longer-term climate commitments.
