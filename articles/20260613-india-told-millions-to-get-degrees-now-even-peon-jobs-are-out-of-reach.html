India told millions to get degrees. Now even peon jobs are out of reach

What happened

India Today reports that India's massive expansion of higher education has created a generation of degree-holders who are now unable to secure even entry-level government positions, with competition for peon and Class IV jobs drawing applications from candidates with bachelor's and master's degrees. The piece frames this as the unintended consequence of a policy push that encouraged degree acquisition without sufficiently developing the economy's capacity to absorb educated workers. The report points to the structural mismatch between the type of education being imparted and the skills demanded by employers in both the public and private sectors. This phenomenon has been documented in state after state, with lakhs of graduates applying for a handful of low-level government vacancies. The story reflects a broader crisis of youth unemployment that is increasingly a flashpoint in Indian politics and society.

The context

India has seen a dramatic expansion in higher education institutions and enrolment over the past two decades, with the Gross Enrolment Ratio in higher education rising significantly. However, the quality of education at many institutions — particularly newer private colleges — has been questioned, with graduates often lacking the practical skills demanded by the job market. India's formal employment sector, including government jobs, has not grown commensurate with the number of graduates entering the workforce each year. The phenomenon of over-qualified candidates applying for low-skill jobs has been repeatedly documented, from UP peon recruitments attracting PhD holders to similar scenes across Bihar, Rajasthan, and other states. Youth unemployment and underemployment are among the most politically sensitive issues ahead of state and national elections.

Why it matters

For millions of young Indians and their families who have invested heavily in education — often taking on debt or selling assets — the failure to find commensurate employment is a direct financial and psychological blow. The mismatch also represents a massive waste of human capital that holds back India's economic growth potential. Politically, a large population of frustrated, educated but unemployed youth is a source of social instability and has been linked to unrest in various parts of the country. Policymakers face pressure to reform both the education system and employment generation strategies to address this structural failure.

What's next

Expect this issue to feature prominently in upcoming state election campaigns and Budget discussions, with pressure on the government to announce job creation schemes and education reforms. Policy experts and industry bodies are likely to step up calls for curriculum overhaul and closer linkages between educational institutions and industry to improve employability.
