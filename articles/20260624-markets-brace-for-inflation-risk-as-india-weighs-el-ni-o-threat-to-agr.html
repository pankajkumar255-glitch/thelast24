Markets brace for inflation risk as India weighs El Niño threat to agriculture

Key facts

  • El Niño conditions flagged as risk to India's monsoon
  • Potential food inflation spike could complicate RBI rate decisions
  • Food has heavy weighting in India's consumer price index
  • Rural consumption sectors most exposed to agricultural shock

Indian markets are beginning to price in a new risk factor: the possibility that an El Niño weather event could undermine the 2025 monsoon, crimp agricultural output and reignite food inflation at a time when policymakers are hoping to ease monetary conditions. The concern is not hypothetical — India's inflation history shows a clear pattern of food price spikes in El Niño years, driven by reduced rainfall in key farming regions.

The implications for monetary policy are significant. The Reserve Bank of India has been cautiously navigating a path toward easier rates, but a sustained surge in food prices — particularly vegetables, pulses and cereals — could force a rethink. Markets are acutely aware that food carries a heavy weight in India's consumer price index, meaning weather-driven supply shocks translate quickly into headline inflation numbers.

For equity markets, the concern plays out in multiple ways. Consumption-oriented sectors — fast-moving consumer goods, rural retail, two-wheelers — are sensitive to rural income shocks, which tend to follow poor harvests. At the same time, higher food inflation can dampen urban consumption by squeezing household budgets.

Meteorological agencies have flagged developing El Niño conditions, though the full impact on the Indian monsoon remains uncertain. Analysts say the market's current posture is one of watchful caution rather than alarm — but if early-season rainfall data disappoints, the inflation risk narrative could move quickly from background concern to front-and-centre market driver.
