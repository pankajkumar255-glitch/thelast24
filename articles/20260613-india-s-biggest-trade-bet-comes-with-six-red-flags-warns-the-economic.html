India's Biggest Trade Bet Comes With Six Red Flags, Warns The Economic Times

What happened

The Economic Times reports that India's most significant ongoing trade negotiation carries six distinct red flags that could compromise the country's economic interests. The piece identifies structural and sectoral vulnerabilities in India's negotiating position, though the specific flags relate to the broader contours of the deal rather than any single clause. The report arrives as Indian officials are actively engaged in trade talks, with Reuters separately confirming that the India-US deal is contingent on a US tariff probe concluding. The Economic Times framing suggests informed caution is warranted even as official optimism about a deal remains high. No specific numerical estimates of losses or gains are cited in the headline.

The context

India has historically been one of the most cautious major economies when it comes to signing free trade agreements, having walked away from the Regional Comprehensive Economic Partnership (RCEP) in 2019 citing risks to domestic industry. The country has since shifted toward a more selective bilateral FTA strategy, concluding agreements with the UAE and Australia and making significant progress on a UK deal. Trade agreements inevitably involve trade-offs: greater market access for Indian exporters abroad in exchange for opening domestic markets to foreign goods and services. India's pharmaceutical, agriculture, dairy, and manufacturing sectors are traditionally sensitive in any trade negotiation. Past FTAs have drawn criticism for benefiting partner countries more than India in certain sectors.

Why it matters

If the risks flagged by The Economic Times materialise, Indian farmers, small manufacturers, and specific industrial sectors could face intensified foreign competition without adequate safeguards. Conversely, poorly structured deals have historically led to trade deficits widening with partner countries. For policymakers, the six red flags serve as a checklist of what negotiators must address before signing. The broader Indian public has a stake because trade deals affect prices, job security in export industries, and the long-term competitiveness of Indian business.

What's next

Expect parliamentary and industry scrutiny of the deal's terms to intensify as negotiations progress, with sector lobbies in agriculture and pharmaceuticals likely to be the most vocal. Analysts will watch whether the government addresses these vulnerabilities in the final text or proceeds despite them.
