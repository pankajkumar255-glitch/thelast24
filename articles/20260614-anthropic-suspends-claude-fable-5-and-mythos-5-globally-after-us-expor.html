Anthropic Suspends Claude Fable 5 and Mythos 5 Globally After US Export Control Directive

What happened

BBC reports that Anthropic has suspended its Claude Fable 5 and Mythos 5 AI models globally for non-US users following a US export control directive tied to national security concerns. NDTV and The Indian Express note that the suspension came within days of the models' launch, making it one of the fastest reversals for a major AI product. Telegraph India reports the suspension is global in scope, affecting users across all non-US geographies simultaneously. Moneycontrol explains that US authorities effectively barred foreigners from accessing these models, invoking export control rules typically applied to sensitive technologies. Indiatimes confirms both Fable 5 and Mythos 5 are affected, with no restoration timeline announced. Deccan Herald reports that the episode has reignited India's self-reliance debate, with Zoho founder Sridhar Vembu publicly stating 'Globalisation Is Dead' in response.

The context

Anthropic, founded in 2021 by former OpenAI researchers including Dario and Daniela Amodei, is one of the leading US AI safety companies and the maker of the Claude family of large language models. The US has increasingly applied export control frameworks — originally designed for semiconductors and defence technologies — to advanced AI models, reflecting growing concerns about technology transfer to strategic rivals. India has a large and fast-growing community of AI developers and startups that rely on API access to frontier models from US companies like Anthropic, OpenAI, and Google. The suspension echoes earlier episodes where US tech policy decisions — such as chip export restrictions — had immediate downstream effects on Indian industry. Sridhar Vembu of Zoho has long advocated for Indian technological self-sufficiency and his comments resonate in a broader national conversation about digital sovereignty.

Why it matters

Indian developers, startups, and enterprises that had integrated Anthropic's most capable models into their products now face an abrupt disruption to their workflows and services. The episode underlines a structural vulnerability: India's AI ecosystem is heavily dependent on models controlled by US companies subject to US government directives. If export controls on AI models become a sustained policy tool, Indian businesses may face repeated, unpredictable access disruptions. The incident is likely to accelerate calls for investment in domestically developed AI models and cloud infrastructure. It also raises costs for Indian firms who may need to rapidly pivot to alternative providers.

What's next

Expect Indian AI startups and developers to urgently evaluate alternative frontier models from providers like Google DeepMind and Meta, whose open-weight models may be less subject to export restrictions. The episode is likely to intensify policy discussions in India around AI sovereignty and the need for homegrown large language model capabilities. Watch for Anthropic to clarify whether and when access may be restored for Indian and other non-US users as it navigates the export control framework.
