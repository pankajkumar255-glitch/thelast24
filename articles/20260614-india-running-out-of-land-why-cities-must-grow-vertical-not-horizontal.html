India Running Out of Land: Why Cities Must Grow Vertical, Not Horizontal

What happened

NDTV reports that India is facing a critical land scarcity challenge in its major cities, arguing that vertical urban growth is no longer optional but essential. The report points to the unsustainability of horizontal urban sprawl given India's land constraints and rising urban population.

The context

India's urban population is projected to reach 600 million by 2030, placing enormous strain on city infrastructure and land availability. Most Indian cities have historically grown outward rather than upward, resulting in sprawling low-density suburbs that strain transport and utility networks. Restrictive Floor Space Index (FSI) regulations in many cities have historically limited vertical construction.

Why it matters

A shift to vertical development could moderate housing prices in cities like Mumbai, Bengaluru, and Delhi over time, making homeownership more accessible. It also has major implications for construction companies, real estate developers, and urban infrastructure investment.

What's next

Expect policy pressure on state governments to revise FSI norms and streamline approvals for high-rise construction as urban land costs continue to rise.
