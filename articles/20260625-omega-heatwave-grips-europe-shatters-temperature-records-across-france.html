'Omega' heatwave grips Europe, shatters temperature records across France, UK and Spain

Key facts

  • Deadly 'Omega' heatwave hitting France, UK and Spain
  • Temperature records shattered across western Europe
  • Blocking atmospheric pattern could prolong the heat event
  • Climate inequality highlighted as poorest households suffer most

A deadly 'Omega' heatwave has descended on western Europe, shattering temperature records across France, the United Kingdom and Spain and prompting urgent warnings from health authorities about the risk to life. The weather system, named for the Greek letter whose shape mirrors the atmospheric blocking pattern trapping hot air over the continent, has created conditions described by residents as unbearable.

In France, the crisis has exposed sharp climate inequality, with those in older, poorly ventilated homes — disproportionately the elderly and low-income — facing the most dangerous conditions. Reports described French residents struggling in heat-trap homes with no access to air conditioning, underlining that extreme weather is not equally distributed across society.

Britain and Spain are also seeing temperatures that have broken historical records, with emergency services stretched and cooling centres opened in major cities. The scale of the event has prompted comparisons to Europe's deadly 2003 heatwave, which killed tens of thousands.

The 'Omega' pattern is particularly concerning because its blocking nature means it resists the normal movement of weather systems, prolonging exposure to dangerous heat. Climate scientists say such events are becoming more frequent and severe due to global warming, and this summer's episode is expected to intensify the debate about how Europe — and the wider world — prepares for a hotter future.
