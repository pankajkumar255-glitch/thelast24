Why Has the India-US Trade Deal Been Delayed? The Hindu Explains

Key facts

  • Tariffs, IP, digital trade, and agriculture are key sticking points
  • India insists on a competitive tariff edge as a precondition
  • US seeks broader market access for agricultural and industrial goods

Even as India and the United States declare willingness to close a trade deal, substantive differences across multiple fronts have kept an agreement out of reach — a situation The Hindu examined in depth in an explainer published on Thursday.

At the heart of the delay are disagreements on tariff structures, with India seeking a competitive edge over rival exporting nations while the US pushes for broader market access for its agricultural and industrial goods. Intellectual property protections, particularly in the pharmaceutical sector, have also been a persistent sticking point — India's generic drug industry has historically resisted US-style IP norms that could raise medicine prices domestically.

Digital trade rules present another layer of complexity, with the US seeking commitments on data flows and e-commerce that Indian policymakers have been reluctant to provide, given domestic concerns about data sovereignty and protecting Indian technology companies. Agriculture is similarly fraught, as India guards its farming sector carefully for reasons of food security and rural livelihoods.

On the political calendar, both governments face domestic pressures that complicate quick compromises. Commerce Minister Piyush Goyal has made clear India's position: no deal unless it delivers a genuine tariff advantage. The Hindu's explainer makes clear that while the intent exists on both sides, the distance between positions remains real — and bridging it will require political will beyond the current negotiating framework.
