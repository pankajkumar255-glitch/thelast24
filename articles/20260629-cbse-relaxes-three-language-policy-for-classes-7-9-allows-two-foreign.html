CBSE Relaxes Three-Language Policy for Classes 7-9, Allows Two Foreign Language Options

Key facts

  • CBSE relaxes three-language policy for Classes 7, 8 and 9
  • Two foreign language options now allowed under the framework
  • Karnataka school body opposes Kannada as third language under CBSE
  • Policy includes a major update for Class 10 students

The Central Board of Secondary Education has notified detailed guidelines for the three-language policy applicable to students in Classes 7, 8 and 9, relaxing earlier provisions to allow two foreign language options within the framework. The circular represents the board's attempt to balance the National Education Policy's language mandate with the practical diversity of its affiliated schools across India.

The policy has immediately run into political resistance in Karnataka, where the state school education body has objected to Kannada being positioned as the third language under the CBSE structure. The objection reflects a long-standing tension between national curriculum frameworks and states' assertions of linguistic identity — a fault line that has repeatedly complicated education policy in southern India.

The guidelines also carry a significant update for Class 10 students, though the specific details of those provisions add nuance to how the policy will be applied at the board examination level. Schools affiliated to CBSE across the country will now need to align their language offerings with the new framework.

The three-language formula has been a contested element of Indian education policy for decades, with disagreements over which languages should be included and in what order. The CBSE's latest notification is likely to trigger further debate between the Centre and state governments, particularly in non-Hindi-speaking states where the formula is viewed with suspicion.
