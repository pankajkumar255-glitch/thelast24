Congress to move Supreme Court over rejection of Meenakshi Natarajan's Rajya Sabha nomination

What happened

The Hindu reports that the Congress party has decided to move the Supreme Court against the rejection of Meenakshi Natarajan's Rajya Sabha nomination, escalating a political dispute into a constitutional legal challenge. Meenakshi Natarajan is a senior Congress leader and former Member of Parliament who was nominated by the party for the Rajya Sabha under the presidential nomination quota. The rejection of her nomination, reportedly by the Rashtrapati Bhavan, has been characterised by Congress as a politically motivated act by the ruling establishment. The party's decision to seek judicial review raises fundamental questions about the scope and limits of the President's discretionary power in nominating members to the upper house. This is an unusual step that reflects the depth of the Congress party's displeasure with the decision.

The context

The Indian Constitution under Article 80 provides for the President to nominate 12 members to the Rajya Sabha from the fields of literature, science, art, and social service. Historically, the President's nomination power has been exercised on the aid and advice of the Council of Ministers — i.e., the Union Cabinet — though the exact constitutional contours have been debated. Opposition parties have periodically alleged that ruling governments use this nomination power to reward allies and exclude opponents. Meenakshi Natarajan served as a Congress MP from Mandsaur in Madhya Pradesh and is a prominent party figure. The rejection of a nomination put forward by an opposition party via the presidential quota route is seen as highly unusual and politically charged.

Why it matters

If the Supreme Court agrees to hear the case, it could be asked to rule definitively on the limits of presidential and governmental discretion in Rajya Sabha nominations, a judgment that would have lasting institutional consequences. A ruling in Congress's favour could constrain future governments from rejecting opposition-backed nominees, strengthening the independence of the nomination process. For the broader political landscape, this case is a flashpoint in the ongoing tension between the ruling BJP government and the Congress-led opposition over control of constitutional institutions. It also raises the profile of Meenakshi Natarajan as a figure around whom the party is willing to mount a high-stakes legal fight.

What's next

Expect the Supreme Court to first decide whether the petition is maintainable — i.e., whether the rejection of a Rajya Sabha nomination is judicially reviewable — before proceeding to the merits. The government is likely to argue that the President's nomination power is a matter of executive discretion not subject to court scrutiny, setting up a significant constitutional debate.
