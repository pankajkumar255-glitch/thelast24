Anthropic Access Ban Reignites India's Self-Reliance Debate; Sridhar Vembu Says 'Globalisation Is Dead'

What happened

Deccan Herald reports that the Anthropic access row has rekindled India's self-reliance debate, with Zoho founder Sridhar Vembu publicly stating that 'Globalisation Is Dead' in response to the US export control directive blocking foreign access to Anthropic's most advanced AI models. Vembu's comments reflect a broader anxiety in Indian technology circles about the risks of building critical systems on foreign AI platforms that can be restricted without notice.

The context

Sridhar Vembu is one of India's most prominent technology entrepreneurs, known for building Zoho into a global software company without venture capital and for his long-standing advocacy of Indian technological independence. India has been investing in domestic AI initiatives, but frontier large language models comparable to those from Anthropic or OpenAI remain largely beyond Indian firms' current capabilities. The Anthropic episode has given fresh urgency to these debates at a time when AI is becoming central to economic competitiveness.

Why it matters

For Indian companies and developers, the episode is a concrete demonstration that access to the world's most powerful AI tools can be revoked overnight for geopolitical reasons, making the case for domestic alternatives more urgent. If India cannot reliably access frontier AI, it risks falling behind in sectors from healthcare to manufacturing that increasingly depend on these technologies.

What's next

Expect louder calls in Indian policy and industry circles for increased government funding and private investment in indigenous large language model development.
