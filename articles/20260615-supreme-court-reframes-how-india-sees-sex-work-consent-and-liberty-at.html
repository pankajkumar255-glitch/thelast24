Supreme Court Reframes How India Sees Sex Work — Consent And Liberty At The Centre

What happened

The Times of India reports that the Supreme Court has issued a ruling on sex work that centres consent and liberty, significantly altering the legal lens through which the profession is viewed in India. The court drew a distinction between voluntary sex work and trafficking, signalling that consenting adults should not be treated as criminals. The judgment is expected to influence how law enforcement, courts, and government policy approach sex workers' rights.

The context

India does not explicitly criminalise sex work itself, but associated activities and frequent police harassment effectively push sex workers into legal grey zones and expose them to abuse. The Supreme Court had previously in 2022 directed that sex workers be treated with dignity and that police not interfere with consenting adults. This latest ruling builds on that trajectory, reinforcing a rights-based approach rooted in constitutional guarantees of liberty and equality.

Why it matters

The ruling could reduce arbitrary police harassment of consenting adult sex workers and strengthen their access to legal protections and social welfare schemes. It also sets a precedent that personal liberty arguments can override morality-based restrictions, with broader implications for civil liberties jurisprudence in India.

What's next

Expect advocacy groups and state governments to reassess rehabilitation and anti-trafficking programmes in light of the ruling, and challenges to associated penal provisions likely to follow in lower courts.
