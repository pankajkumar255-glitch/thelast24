India Launches World's First Nuclear-Powered Hydrogen Production Facility

Key facts

  • World's first hydrogen production facility using nuclear technology
  • Launched in India, ahead of any other nation
  • Combines India's nuclear programme with green hydrogen ambitions
  • Potential to decarbonise heavy industry and energy storage

India has commissioned the world's first hydrogen production facility powered by nuclear technology, placing the country at the forefront of a global clean energy race. The facility marks a landmark moment not just for India's atomic energy sector but for the emerging green hydrogen economy worldwide, as no other nation has previously deployed nuclear power specifically for hydrogen production at this scale.

The project reflects a strategic convergence of two of India's major long-term priorities: expanding its nuclear energy programme and building a robust green hydrogen ecosystem. Hydrogen produced via nuclear power avoids the carbon emissions associated with conventional hydrogen generation methods, making it one of the cleanest possible pathways to the fuel. India has been investing heavily in hydrogen as a replacement for fossil fuels in heavy industry, transport, and energy storage.

The development is significant given India's ambitious clean energy targets and its stated goal of becoming a major hydrogen exporter. By being the first mover in nuclear-powered hydrogen, India gains a technological and strategic edge, potentially attracting global investment and partnerships in this nascent sector. The Department of Atomic Energy and allied research institutions are understood to be central to the project.

Energy analysts note that nuclear-powered hydrogen sidesteps one of the key limitations of renewable-based green hydrogen — the intermittency of solar and wind power — by providing a continuous, high-temperature heat source for electrolysis or thermochemical processes. If scaled successfully, this facility could serve as a model for other countries looking to decarbonise hard-to-abate industries. India's first-mover status here is a rare and consequential technological distinction on the world stage.
