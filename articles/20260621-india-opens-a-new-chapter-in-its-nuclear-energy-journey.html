India Opens a New Chapter in Its Nuclear Energy Journey

Key facts

  • India signalling new policy phase for nuclear energy expansion
  • Nuclear power positioned as key clean baseload alternative to coal
  • Follows 2008 Indo-US deal and agreements with France, Russia, Japan
  • Aims to meet net-zero targets and surging electricity demand

India's nuclear energy programme is entering a new phase of expansion, with the government signalling fresh policy intent to scale up atomic power capacity as part of its long-term energy security strategy. The push comes as India grapples with record electricity demand, ambitious renewable targets, and the need to reduce dependence on coal — a fuel source that accounts for the bulk of the country's power generation but carries severe environmental costs.

Nuclear power currently contributes a modest share of India's total electricity output, but the government has long identified it as a critical baseload option capable of delivering large volumes of clean, round-the-clock power that solar and wind cannot reliably provide on their own. The new chapter referenced by PIB is expected to involve accelerating the construction of new reactors, revisiting the legal framework around private participation, and deepening international civil nuclear partnerships.

India's civil nuclear ambitions received a landmark boost from the 2008 Indo-US nuclear deal, which ended India's decades-long isolation from global nuclear commerce. Since then, agreements with France, Russia, Japan and others have laid the groundwork for new reactor projects, though land acquisition, regulatory timelines, and financing challenges have repeatedly slowed progress. The government's renewed push signals an attempt to cut through those bottlenecks.

For ordinary Indians, the stakes are tangible: cheaper, cleaner baseload power could moderate long-run electricity tariffs and meaningfully reduce the coal-fired pollution that blankets major cities. Whether this latest announcement translates into actual reactor construction at scale will be the test that matters.
