The Hindu explains: Why India has upgraded its core statistical databases

Key facts

  • India has upgraded its official statistical databases for economic data
  • Aims to align GDP, inflation and employment data with international standards
  • Responds to longstanding criticism of gaps and inconsistencies in official data
  • Better data expected to improve policy accuracy and investor confidence

India has upgraded its core statistical databases in a wide-ranging overhaul intended to sharpen the accuracy, coverage and international comparability of the country's official economic data. The move addresses longstanding criticisms that India's data collection systems were outdated, patchy in coverage and sometimes slow to reflect ground realities.

The upgrade touches the foundations of how India measures its economy — including the methodologies underpinning GDP estimates, consumer price indices, employment surveys and industrial output figures. Aligning these systems with frameworks recommended by international bodies such as the United Nations and the International Monetary Fund is a core objective, making India's statistics more legible to global investors and multilateral agencies.

Critics and independent economists have for years raised concerns about the reliability of India's official data, pointing to discrepancies between survey-based employment numbers and payroll data, as well as debates over the base-year for GDP calculations. The database upgrade is, in part, a response to these concerns — an acknowledgement that the country's statistical architecture needed modernisation to keep pace with a rapidly transforming economy.

For policymakers, better data means better-calibrated interventions — whether in monetary policy, fiscal transfers or welfare targeting. For businesses and investors, improved timeliness and accuracy reduce uncertainty. The Hindu's deep-dive into the rationale for the changes comes at a moment when the credibility of official statistics has become a subject of genuine public debate in India.
