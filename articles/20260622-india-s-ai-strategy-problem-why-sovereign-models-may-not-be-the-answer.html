India's AI Strategy Problem: Why Sovereign Models May Not Be the Answer

Key facts

  • India AI Mission involves significant public spending on domestic AI
  • Sovereign model-building requires massive compute and talent investment
  • Analysis recommends focusing on AI applications and governance instead
  • Debate centres on how India allocates its national AI strategy resources

India's ambition to build sovereign large language models may be pulling the country's AI strategy in the wrong direction, Business Standard argues in a sharp critique of current policy thinking. The core concern is that developing frontier AI models from scratch demands extraordinary investment in compute infrastructure, world-class research talent, and years of sustained effort — resources that even the wealthiest nations struggle to marshal effectively.

The argument is not that India should abandon AI ambition, but that the framing of "sovereignty" around model-building conflates national pride with strategic utility. Countries that have attempted to build purely homegrown frontier models have often found the results lag well behind models developed by OpenAI, Google DeepMind, and Anthropic, raising questions about value for public money.

A more productive path, the analysis suggests, would be to focus India's energy on building AI applications atop existing frontier models, developing robust data governance frameworks, training a large domestic AI-ready workforce, and ensuring Indian languages and contexts are well-represented in global models. These goals are achievable at far lower cost and could yield real economic returns more quickly.

The debate arrives at a critical moment, as the Indian government weighs significant public expenditure on AI under its India AI Mission. How policymakers respond to this challenge will determine whether India carves out a distinctive and competitive role in the global AI economy or finds itself outpaced by both Western labs and China.
