Indian Economy Cannot Outrun Heat, Hindustan Times Warns

What happened

Hindustan Times reports that the Indian economy faces a growing and unavoidable drag from extreme heat, arguing that rising temperatures impose costs on labour productivity, agricultural output, and public health that cannot be outrun by GDP growth alone. The report frames heat not as a seasonal inconvenience but as a structural economic risk. It comes against a backdrop of India experiencing intense heatwaves across multiple states in recent seasons. The publication does not suggest an immediate crisis but warns that the compounding nature of climate-linked heat stress will constrain India's growth potential over time.

The context

India is among the world's most climate-vulnerable large economies, with hundreds of millions of people dependent on rain-fed agriculture and outdoor labour. The country has seen a marked increase in the frequency, duration, and intensity of heatwaves over the past decade, with temperatures exceeding 45°C across large parts of north and central India in recent summers. The economic literature on heat and productivity shows that labour output falls sharply above certain temperature thresholds, particularly for outdoor and construction workers. India's National Action Plan on Climate Change acknowledges heat as a key risk, but implementation of adaptation measures has been uneven.

Why it matters

For the roughly 200 million Indians employed in agriculture and the many more in construction and informal outdoor work, extreme heat directly reduces earning days and physical capacity. Crop yield losses from heat stress raise food prices and hit rural incomes simultaneously. If heat-linked productivity losses are not accounted for in planning, India's headline GDP growth figures may overstate genuine improvements in living standards. Investors in sectors exposed to outdoor labour and agriculture face rising climate risk that is not yet fully priced in.

What's next

Expect greater policy focus on heat action plans at the state level and potential pressure on the Union government to integrate climate adaptation more formally into economic planning. Corporate India, particularly in construction and logistics, will likely face growing scrutiny over worker welfare during extreme heat events.
