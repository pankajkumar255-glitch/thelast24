Why crude oil, monsoon and fertiliser prices are worrying the government

What happened

India Today reports that government officials are closely monitoring crude oil prices, the monsoon's progress, and fertiliser costs, as all three pose simultaneous risks to India's macroeconomic stability and farm sector. The intersection of these pressures is particularly acute heading into the kharif crop season.

The context

India is a major importer of crude oil and fertilisers, and a significant portion of fertiliser subsidies is linked to global gas and naphtha prices. The monsoon is the lifeline of Indian agriculture, which supports nearly half the workforce. A bad monsoon combined with high input costs can trigger food inflation and force the government to expand subsidy expenditure, straining the fiscal deficit.

Why it matters

Rising fertiliser and fuel costs translate directly into higher food prices for urban consumers and squeezed margins for farmers, while a weak monsoon compounds food inflation risks. The government may face pressure to increase subsidies or cut fuel taxes, both of which affect public finances.

What's next

Expect the government to closely track the India Meteorological Department's monsoon forecasts over the next four to six weeks and potentially review fertiliser subsidy allocations ahead of the kharif sowing season.
