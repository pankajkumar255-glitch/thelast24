Why crude oil, monsoon and fertiliser prices are worrying the government

What happened

India Today reports that the government is concerned about the interplay of crude oil prices, monsoon performance, and fertiliser costs. All three are sources of fiscal and inflationary pressure that could complicate economic management in the months ahead. The report comes as global oil markets remain volatile and the monsoon season gets underway.

The context

India subsidises both fuel and fertilisers heavily, so rising global prices for crude and fertiliser inputs directly inflate the government's subsidy bill. The monsoon, which irrigates over half of India's farmland, determines agricultural output, food prices, and rural consumer spending. A poor monsoon combined with high input costs is a classic recipe for food inflation.

Why it matters

If all three risks materialise together, consumers could face higher food and fuel prices while the government struggles to keep subsidies affordable — a squeeze felt hardest by lower-income households. It also limits the Reserve Bank of India's ability to cut interest rates to support growth.

What's next

Expect the government to closely monitor June-July monsoon arrival data and global crude benchmarks before deciding on any revision to fuel or fertiliser subsidy allocations.
