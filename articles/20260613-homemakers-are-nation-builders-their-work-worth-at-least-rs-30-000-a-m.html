Homemakers are 'nation builders', their work worth at least Rs 30,000 a month: Supreme Court

What happened

The Hindu and India Today both report that the Supreme Court has declared homemakers to be 'nation builders' and held that their domestic labour is worth at least Rs 30,000 per month. The ruling addresses the longstanding legal problem of quantifying unpaid household work, which courts have often struggled to value fairly. The judgment is expected to set a precedent for how compensation is calculated in motor accident claims and other cases involving homemakers. India Today NE also covered the ruling, underscoring its broad national significance. The bench's language was notably affirmative, framing domestic work not as supplementary but as foundational to the nation's functioning.

The context

Indian courts have long grappled with how to assign monetary value to the work of homemakers, especially in cases under the Motor Vehicles Act where families seek compensation after a homemaker's death or disability. Previous judgments have used varying and often low benchmarks, leading to inconsistent and frequently inadequate awards. The Supreme Court has periodically revisited this issue, with earlier rulings nudging tribunals to account for domestic contribution more seriously. Women's rights groups and legal scholars have long argued that the invisibility of domestic labour in economic accounting perpetuates gender inequality. This ruling is among the most explicit and quantified statements the apex court has made on the subject.

Why it matters

For millions of Indian families where one member — almost always a woman — manages the household full-time, this ruling establishes a concrete financial floor for their contribution in legal proceedings. It could raise compensation payouts in motor accident tribunal cases significantly, directly benefiting families who have lost a homemaker or whose homemaker has been disabled. The ruling also carries symbolic weight, formally challenging the idea that only paid employment constitutes economically meaningful work. Over time, it may influence insurance product design and even broader policy conversations around care work.

What's next

Expect motor accident claims tribunals across India to begin applying the Rs 30,000 monthly benchmark in fresh and pending compensation cases. Legal challenges and appeals around the precise application of the figure are likely, as insurance companies and opposing parties test the ruling's scope. Advocacy groups may push for the judgment to inform broader policy, including social security schemes for homemakers.
