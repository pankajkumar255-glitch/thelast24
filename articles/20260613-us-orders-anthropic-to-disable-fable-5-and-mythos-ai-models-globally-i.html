US orders Anthropic to disable Fable 5 and Mythos AI models globally; Indian firms sound alarm

What happened

The Economic Times reports that the US government ordered Anthropic to disable its Fable 5 and Mythos AI models, with India Inc raising alarms over the sudden disruption. Telegraph India reports that Anthropic has suspended both models globally following a US export control directive tied to security concerns. India Today explains that the ban extends to all foreign nationals, including Indians, blocking access to these specific Claude-family models. The move is framed as an export control measure, meaning these models are now treated similarly to restricted defence or dual-use technologies.

The context

Anthropic is one of the leading US-based AI safety companies, backed by billions in investment and known for its Claude family of large language models. Export control laws in the US, historically applied to semiconductors and military hardware, are increasingly being extended to advanced AI systems as geopolitical competition over AI intensifies. India has a large and growing base of AI developers, startups, and enterprises that rely on American AI platforms, having limited homegrown alternatives at the frontier level. The move echoes earlier US restrictions on advanced chip exports to India and other countries, signalling a broader trend of AI being treated as a strategic national asset.

Why it matters

Indian startups, IT firms, and developers who built products or internal tools on Anthropic's Fable 5 and Mythos models now face immediate operational disruption. This exposes a structural vulnerability: Indian businesses have little recourse when a foreign government unilaterally pulls access to critical AI infrastructure. The episode will likely accelerate calls for India to develop and mandate sovereign AI models, reducing dependence on US platforms. For everyday knowledge workers using these tools, productivity tools may simply stop functioning overnight with no warning.

What's next

Expect Indian industry bodies and the IT ministry to formally raise the issue with the US side, while affected companies scramble to migrate workloads to alternative models such as OpenAI's GPT series or Google's Gemini. The episode is likely to fast-track government discussions around India's own sovereign AI model programme and data localisation norms.
