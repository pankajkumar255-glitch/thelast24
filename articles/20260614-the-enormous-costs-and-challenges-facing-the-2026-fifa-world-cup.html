The Enormous Costs and Challenges Facing the 2026 FIFA World Cup

What happened

BBC reports on the mounting costs and challenges associated with the 2026 FIFA World Cup, which is set to be held across the United States, Canada, and Mexico. The piece examines the scale of the tournament, which for the first time features 48 participating nations, making it the biggest World Cup ever staged. Infrastructure, security, transportation, and cost overruns are flagged as key concerns for the three host nations. The report comes as the tournament is in its preparatory stages, with matches already beginning.

The context

The 2026 FIFA World Cup was awarded jointly to the US, Canada, and Mexico in 2018, marking the first time three nations will co-host the tournament. The expansion from 32 to 48 teams was approved by FIFA to boost global participation and revenue. The US last hosted the World Cup in 1994, which remains one of the most commercially successful tournaments in history. However, critics have raised concerns about the enormous distances between host cities, the cost of stadium upgrades, and the logistical challenge of coordinating security and transportation across borders. Scotland and Brazil are among teams preparing for their opening matches.

Why it matters

With Indian football viewership and fandom growing rapidly, the World Cup's commercial and organisational success directly shapes the global football ecosystem that Indian fans, broadcasters, and advertisers are investing in. Cost overruns at mega-events are a global cautionary tale, and how the US, Canada, and Mexico manage this tournament will set the template for future large-scale sporting events, including potential bids from Asia.

What's next

Expect ongoing coverage of logistical developments as the group stages unfold, with any significant incidents or cost revelations likely to draw further scrutiny from FIFA and host governments.
