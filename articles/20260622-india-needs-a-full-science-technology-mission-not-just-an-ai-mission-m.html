India needs a full Science & Technology Mission, not just an AI mission, Moneycontrol argues

Key facts

  • Moneycontrol commentary urges a broad Science & Technology Mission for India
  • Current IndiaAI Mission seen as too narrow without foundational S&T investment
  • India's R&D spending and basic research infrastructure remain limited
  • Call for DARPA-style coordination linking academia, industry and government

India's ambition to become a global artificial intelligence powerhouse risks falling short if the government does not anchor its AI push within a much broader Science and Technology Mission, according to a sharply argued commentary published by Moneycontrol. The piece makes the case that AI is, at its core, a downstream application of deep scientific capability — and that capability is precisely what India has underinvested in for decades.

The commentary points to India's relatively modest public spending on research and development, its limited number of world-class research institutions outside a handful of IITs and IIScs, and a talent pipeline that is strong in software services but thinner in fundamental research. Without addressing these structural gaps, it argues, India's AI mission risks being dependent on imported models, foreign chips and global platforms rather than generating indigenous innovation.

The argument is timely. The Indian government has announced the IndiaAI Mission with a significant budget allocation, and global technology giants are queuing up to build data centres and partner with Indian institutions. But critics, including the Moneycontrol commentary, warn that framing the national technology agenda primarily around AI misses the forest for the trees — semiconductors, quantum computing, space technology, biotechnology and advanced materials all require a foundational scientific base that a single-sector mission cannot build.

The piece calls for a national-level coordination body that integrates AI with broader S&T goals, linking academia, industry and government in a manner analogous to DARPA in the United States. Whether the government's current institutional architecture is equipped to deliver that ambition remains an open question.
