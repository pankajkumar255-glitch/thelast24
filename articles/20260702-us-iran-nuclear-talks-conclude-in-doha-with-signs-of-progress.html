US-Iran Nuclear Talks Conclude in Doha With Signs of Progress

Key facts

  • Talks concluded in Doha, Qatar, focused on the Strait of Hormuz
  • Trump said US and Iran are 'getting along very well'
  • Iran agreed to open a communication channel on an MoU with the US
  • Significant gaps on the nuclear file remain between the two sides

US and Iranian negotiators concluded a fresh round of nuclear diplomacy in Doha, Qatar, with both sides signalling cautious but real movement toward a possible agreement. President Donald Trump, speaking publicly on the talks, said the two countries were "getting along very well" and expressed confidence that progress was being made — striking a notably warmer tone than has characterised US-Iran relations in recent years.

Iran, for its part, said it would open a communication channel on a memorandum of understanding with Washington following the Qatar round, suggesting the talks produced at least a procedural framework for continued engagement. The discussions were focused in part on the Strait of Hormuz, the critical waterway through which a significant share of the world's oil supply passes — underlining the geopolitical stakes well beyond the nuclear question alone.

The path to a deal remains uncertain. Iranian officials signalled constraints on how directly their delegation could engage with American counterparts, and significant gaps on the nuclear file are known to remain. Still, the Doha talks mark one of the most substantive diplomatic exchanges between the two countries in years, following the collapse of the 2015 JCPOA nuclear accord.

For India, which imports a substantial portion of its crude oil from the Gulf region and once relied heavily on Iranian oil before US sanctions, any durable agreement carries major economic implications. A reduction in regional tensions could ease oil-supply anxieties, while a potential sanctions rollback on Iran could reopen energy options for New Delhi.
