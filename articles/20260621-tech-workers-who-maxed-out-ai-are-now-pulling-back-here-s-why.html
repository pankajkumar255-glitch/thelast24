Tech workers who maxed out AI are now pulling back — here's why

Key facts

  • Heavy AI users in tech are deliberately reducing their AI tool usage
  • Key concerns: skill erosion, over-dependence, unreliable AI output
  • Trend affects software, writing, and knowledge-work professionals
  • Shift signals move from uncritical adoption to selective, deliberate use

Tech workers who were once among the most enthusiastic adopters of AI tools are now actively trying to minimise their use — a quiet but telling reversal that signals a maturing, more sceptical relationship with the technology.

The trend, reported by The Indian Express, captures a mood shift among professionals in software, content, and knowledge-intensive fields who had pushed AI assistance to its limits. Many are now pulling back over concerns that heavy reliance on tools like ChatGPT and GitHub Copilot is eroding core skills — coding instincts, writing ability, critical thinking — that took years to develop. The quality of AI output, which can be confidently wrong, has also contributed to growing wariness.

The pattern matters because tech workers were supposed to be AI's most willing converts. Their disillusionment does not mean AI adoption is reversing, but it suggests the initial wave of uncritical enthusiasm is giving way to deliberate, selective use. Workers are drawing their own lines around which tasks they trust to AI and which they insist on doing themselves.

For India, where a large chunk of the working population is employed in IT services and increasingly AI-adjacent roles, the debate is acutely relevant. As companies push AI-first workflows, the question of what happens to the expertise of the humans in the loop is becoming harder to ignore. The backlash from early adopters may be the first real signal that the industry needs guardrails — not just on AI safety, but on human skill preservation.
