'My Founder Kept Me Awake Till 4 AM Before an Exam': The Indian vs US Startup Culture Debate

Key facts

  • Engineer kept awake till 4 AM by founder before a personal exam
  • Post went viral, triggering Indian vs US startup culture comparison
  • Debate centres on founder entitlement and lack of professional boundaries
  • Part of a broader recurring conversation about Indian startup work culture

A software engineer's first-person account of being kept awake until 4 AM by a startup founder on the eve of a personal exam has gone viral, touching a nerve among India's millions of young tech workers and sparking a pointed comparison between the working cultures of Indian and US startups. The account, which circulated widely on social media before being picked up by Hindustan Times, describes a pattern many workers recognise: founders treating employees' personal time, health, and commitments as subordinate to the company's immediate demands.

The contrast with US startup culture — where, at least in popular perception, professional boundaries are more formally observed and employees are more willing to push back on unreasonable demands — has generated extensive debate online. Many respondents to the original post shared similar experiences of being messaged late at night, expected to be available on weekends, or pressured to deprioritise personal milestones for work.

Defenders of India's intense startup culture argue that the high-pressure environment reflects genuine competitive stakes and a shared-sacrifice ethos that has driven some of the country's most successful companies. Critics counter that what gets labelled 'hustle culture' is often simply poor management, founder insecurity, and the exploitation of a job market where employees fear speaking up.

The episode is the latest in a recurring public conversation about workplace norms in India's startup world — one that has previously surfaced around comments by prominent founders about 90-hour work weeks and hiring people with 'nothing else going on in their lives.' As India's tech workforce grows and matures, pressure is building on startup founders to distinguish between genuine commitment and simple overreach.
