Trump Threatens to Hit Iran 'Very Hard Tonight' and Seize Control of Its Oil

What happened

NDTV reports that US President Donald Trump publicly threatened to hit Iran 'very hard tonight' and take Venezuela-like control of its oil if negotiations over Iran's nuclear programme do not yield a deal. This followed an earlier NDTV report, published at 00:48 IST the same day, stating that Iran had not yet approved any deal with the United States. Trump's reference to Venezuela-style oil control alludes to US sanctions and pressure campaigns that have severely curtailed Venezuelan oil exports. The back-to-back reports suggest negotiations between Washington and Tehran remain at an impasse, with Trump openly threatening military and economic consequences.

The context

The United States and Iran have been locked in a nuclear standoff since Trump withdrew from the 2015 Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action (JCPOA) during his first term and reimposed sweeping sanctions. Iran has steadily increased its uranium enrichment levels in response, with international watchdogs warning that Tehran is closer than ever to weapons-grade enrichment capacity. Trump returned to the presidency in January 2025 with a stated policy of maximum pressure on Iran. Indirect and direct talks have taken place in 2025, but fundamental gaps over enrichment rights and sanctions relief remain. India imports significant quantities of oil and has historically maintained trade and diplomatic ties with Iran, making any conflict or sanctions tightening directly consequential for New Delhi.

Why it matters

Any US military strike on Iran would almost certainly cause a sharp spike in global crude oil prices, directly raising petrol, diesel, and LPG costs for Indian consumers. India's strategic interests in the region, including the Chabahar port project and energy imports, would be jeopardised by an armed conflict. Heightened tensions also risk broader Middle East instability, threatening the safety of the nearly nine million Indians living and working in the Gulf region. Investor sentiment in Indian markets would also be rattled by a war premium on oil.

What's next

Expect urgent diplomatic activity from regional powers and European nations to de-escalate tensions in the coming 24-48 hours. India is likely to monitor developments closely given its Chabahar port stakes and oil import interests. If no deal materialises, markets should brace for crude price volatility in the near term.
