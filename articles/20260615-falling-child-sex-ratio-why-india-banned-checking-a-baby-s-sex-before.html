Falling Child Sex Ratio: Why India Banned Checking a Baby's Sex Before Birth

What happened

The Indian Express reports on the persistent decline in India's child sex ratio and examines the rationale behind the PCPNDT Act, which bans sex determination of a foetus. The report highlights systemic bias as a continuing driver of sex-selective practices despite the legal prohibition, and contextualises why the ban remains necessary decades after its introduction.

The context

India banned prenatal sex determination through the PCPNDT Act in 1994 after census data revealed alarming declines in the female child population, particularly in states like Haryana, Punjab, and Rajasthan. Despite the law, enforcement has been patchy, and technological advances have made illegal sex determination easier to conduct covertly. The 2011 census recorded a child sex ratio of 914 girls per 1,000 boys, among the lowest globally, and recent data from several states shows the trend has not fully reversed.

Why it matters

A declining child sex ratio has long-term consequences for Indian society — from increased violence against women to distorted marriage markets — making effective enforcement of the PCPNDT Act a matter of national demographic health.

What's next

Expect renewed calls from health advocates and policymakers for stricter enforcement mechanisms and updated penalties under the PCPNDT Act in light of the latest sex ratio data.
