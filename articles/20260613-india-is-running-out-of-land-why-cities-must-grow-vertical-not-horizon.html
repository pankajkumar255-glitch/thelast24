India is running out of land: why cities must grow vertical, not horizontal

What happened

NDTV reports that India is facing a structural shortage of urban land as its cities continue to expand rapidly, and argues that the solution lies in vertical growth — higher-density, high-rise development — rather than continued horizontal sprawl into periurban and agricultural land. The piece frames this as an urgent economic and planning challenge, noting that land scarcity is a primary driver of unaffordable housing in major Indian metros. Urban land prices in cities like Mumbai, Delhi, and Bengaluru have made homeownership increasingly out of reach for middle-income households. The report calls for regulatory and policy changes to enable denser construction.

The context

India adds tens of millions of urban residents every decade, making it one of the fastest urbanising countries in the world. Floor Space Index (FSI) regulations — which cap how much built area is permitted per unit of land — have historically been restrictive in Indian cities compared to global peers, limiting vertical development even where demand is acute. The government's Housing for All and Smart Cities missions have sought to address affordability and urban infrastructure, but land acquisition and zoning remain major bottlenecks. Mumbai and Delhi in particular have some of the world's highest land prices relative to incomes, partly as a result of low FSI norms.

Why it matters

Unaffordable housing locks urban workers — especially migrants and young professionals — into inadequate or informal accommodation, reducing productivity and quality of life. Encouraging vertical growth could, if accompanied by infrastructure investment, bring down per-unit housing costs and make homeownership accessible to more Indians. For real estate investors and developers, a regulatory shift toward higher FSI would reshape the entire sector's economics and land acquisition strategies.

What's next

Expect urban planning reform — particularly FSI liberalisation — to feature in state government policy debates and potentially in central government initiatives linked to housing affordability. Cities that move early on permitting denser development are likely to attract greater private real estate investment.
