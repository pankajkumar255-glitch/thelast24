India's Biggest Trade Bet Comes With Six Red Flags, Warns Economic Times

What happened

The Economic Times reports that India's biggest trade bet — its current trade negotiation strategy, most prominently with the United States — carries six distinct red flags that policymakers and businesses need to reckon with. The publication's analysis identifies these risks as material threats to the expected benefits of the deal, though the specific six risks are detailed within the full article. The report frames this as a critical moment of reckoning for India's trade policy as the country positions itself to benefit from global supply chain shifts. The analysis arrives as multiple reports confirm that a US trade law and an ongoing tariff probe are already delaying the deal's conclusion.

The context

India has been aggressively pursuing bilateral trade agreements as part of its strategy to attract manufacturing investment and boost exports, with the US deal being the most high-profile. India has also been negotiating with the UK, the EU, and Gulf countries. Trade agreements carry inherent risks — they require opening domestic markets to foreign competition, which can threaten sectors like agriculture, dairy, and small manufacturing. India's previous reluctance to join the RCEP regional trade bloc was partly driven by fear of import surges, particularly from China. Balancing export ambition with protecting vulnerable domestic sectors is a recurring tension in India's trade policy.

Why it matters

If the red flags identified by The Economic Times go unaddressed, India could end up with a trade deal that benefits large exporters while exposing farmers, small manufacturers, and certain services sectors to damaging foreign competition. Getting the deal's terms right matters enormously for millions of livelihoods and for India's long-term industrial strategy. A flawed agreement could also lock in unfavourable terms for years, given how difficult trade deals are to renegotiate once signed.

What's next

Expect civil society groups, industry associations, and opposition parties to amplify these concerns as trade negotiations progress. Watch for official government responses that either address the flagged risks or indicate which sectors will receive protected treatment in any final agreement.
