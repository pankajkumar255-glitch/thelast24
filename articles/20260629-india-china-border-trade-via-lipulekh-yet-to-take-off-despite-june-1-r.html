India-China border trade via Lipulekh yet to take off despite June 1 reopening

Key facts

  • Lipulekh border pass reopened for India-China trade on June 1
  • No meaningful trade activity has occurred since reopening
  • Pass is located in Uttarakhand, a historically significant trade route
  • Closure stemmed from post-2020 Galwan-era India-China tensions

The Lipulekh border pass between India and China, reopened for trade on June 1, has yet to see any meaningful commercial activity, according to The Times of India — a slow start that underscores how difficult it is to rebuild economic ties disrupted by years of military standoff and diplomatic chill.

Lipulekh, situated in Uttarakhand's Pithoragarh district, is one of India's historically significant overland trade routes with Tibet and China. The pass was effectively closed following the deterioration in bilateral relations after the Galwan Valley clash of 2020, which resulted in casualties on both sides and a prolonged military standoff along the Line of Actual Control. Its formal reopening is part of the broader diplomatic reset being cautiously pursued by both New Delhi and Beijing.

Despite the official restart, traders and local businesses have not yet moved goods through the route. Practical barriers — including logistics infrastructure, customs procedures, and the trust deficit accumulated over years of hostility — appear to be slowing any revival. Border communities in the region, who historically benefited from trade with Tibetan counterparts in goods including wool, salt, and local produce, remain in a holding pattern.

The Lipulekh experience mirrors a broader pattern in India-China normalisation: diplomatic agreements and formal reopenings move faster than on-the-ground reality. Whether trade picks up in the coming weeks will be an early practical test of how durable the current thaw between Asia's two largest nations truly is.
