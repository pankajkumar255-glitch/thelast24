Trump Says 'I Love the Inflation' as Tariff Impact Bites US Economy

What happened

BBC reports that US President Donald Trump declared 'I love the inflation' in public remarks, a statement that stands in stark contrast to his campaign rhetoric blaming the previous administration for rising prices. Trump's tariff regime, which includes broad levies on imports from multiple countries, has been flagged by economists and business groups as a key driver of inflationary pressure in the United States. The comment comes amid ongoing debate about the economic impact of his trade policies since returning to office in January 2025. BBC published the story at 22:35 IST.

The context

Inflation in the United States surged to four-decade highs in 2022-23 under President Biden, and Trump made the cost of living a cornerstone of his successful 2024 campaign. Upon returning to office, Trump imposed a sweeping set of tariffs on goods from China, the European Union, and other trading partners as part of his 'America First' trade agenda. Economists have warned that tariffs act as a tax on imports, raising prices for consumers. The US Federal Reserve has kept interest rates at elevated levels to fight inflation, which has global ripple effects including on emerging markets like India.

Why it matters

Persistently high US inflation, if endorsed or worsened by Trump's policies, keeps US interest rates elevated, pulling foreign capital away from emerging markets like India and weakening the rupee. A weaker rupee makes imports — particularly oil — more expensive for Indian businesses and consumers. Indian IT and export companies that earn in dollars but report in rupees face currency unpredictability. The broader signal of a US president comfortable with inflation is unsettling for global financial markets that have spent years trying to tame it.

What's next

Expect financial markets to closely scrutinise upcoming US inflation data and Federal Reserve commentary for signs of policy response to Trump's tariff-driven price pressures. India's Reserve Bank will likely keep a close watch on rupee movement and capital outflow trends in this environment.
