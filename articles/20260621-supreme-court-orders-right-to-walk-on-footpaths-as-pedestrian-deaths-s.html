Supreme Court orders right to walk on footpaths as pedestrian deaths surge across India

Key facts

  • Supreme Court affirmed the right to walk on footpaths as a legal entitlement
  • Ruling driven by a documented surge in pedestrian deaths across India
  • State governments and municipal bodies now under pressure to maintain encroachment-free footpaths
  • Order can underpin future PILs or contempt action against non-compliant authorities

The Supreme Court of India has issued a significant order affirming the right of citizens to walk on footpaths, responding to mounting evidence of a dangerous surge in pedestrian deaths on Indian roads. The ruling places the onus on state governments and urban local bodies to ensure footpaths are built, maintained, and kept free from encroachment — framing safe walking as a legal entitlement rather than an infrastructure afterthought.

The backdrop to the order is deeply troubling. India consistently records among the highest rates of pedestrian road fatalities in the world, with a significant proportion of road deaths involving people on foot. In many Indian cities, footpaths are either non-existent, crumbling, or routinely occupied by vendors, vehicles, and construction material, forcing pedestrians onto busy carriageways.

The Indian Express reported that the Court's intervention was directly prompted by data reflecting this surge in pedestrian deaths, signalling judicial impatience with the pace of urban infrastructure reform. The order is expected to serve as a basis for contempt proceedings or public interest litigation against municipal authorities that fail to comply.

The ruling has immediate implications for city planners, traffic authorities, and state governments, who will now face judicial scrutiny over the condition of pedestrian infrastructure. For ordinary Indians — particularly in dense urban areas — the order represents a rare moment of legal recognition that getting from one place to another safely on foot is a right the state must guarantee.
