Iran Has Not Yet Approved Any Nuclear Deal With the US, Reports Say

What happened

NDTV reported at 00:48 IST that Iran has not yet approved any deal with the United States, citing reports on the state of ongoing nuclear negotiations between Washington and Tehran. The report provided no indication of a breakthrough being imminent. This report preceded and contextualised a far more aggressive statement from US President Donald Trump later in the same day, in which Trump threatened to strike Iran and take control of its oil. Taken together, the two NDTV reports paint a picture of stalled diplomacy and escalating US pressure on Iran.

The context

US-Iran nuclear negotiations have been on and off since Trump withdrew from the 2015 JCPOA during his first term and reimposed maximum pressure sanctions. Iran responded by steadily ramping up uranium enrichment, and by 2025 was assessed to be enriching uranium to levels close to weapons-grade. Trump returned to office in January 2025 promising to conclude a new, stricter deal or face consequences. Back-channel and indirect talks have been reported in 2025, but the two sides remain far apart on core issues including Iran's right to enrich uranium domestically and the scope and sequencing of sanctions relief.

Why it matters

A continuing impasse raises the probability of military confrontation, which would send oil prices sharply higher and directly burden Indian consumers and the Indian economy. India has strategic interests in Iran through the Chabahar port — a key connectivity project linking India to Afghanistan and Central Asia — which would be jeopardised by conflict. The millions of Indians working in Gulf countries neighbouring Iran would also be at risk in the event of a broader regional conflict.

What's next

Diplomatic back-channels will likely remain active despite Trump's threats, with Oman and other intermediaries expected to continue facilitating contacts. India will likely monitor the situation closely given its Chabahar commitments and energy import needs, and may quietly engage both sides diplomatically.
