Supreme Court Reshapes India's View on Sex Work, Consent and Personal Liberty

What happened

The Times of India reports that the Supreme Court has issued a ruling that changes the way India's legal system views sex work, emphasising consent and liberty as central principles. The court's observations draw a clear distinction between voluntary adult sex work and forced exploitation, and direct authorities to treat consensual sex workers with dignity rather than as criminals. The ruling builds on earlier Supreme Court directions that recognised sex workers' rights to basic entitlements.

The context

India does not have a single law that straightforwardly criminalises or legalises sex work; the Immoral Traffic (Prevention) Act regulates related activities in ways that often expose sex workers to police abuse. The Supreme Court has previously, in 2022, directed that police must not harass or arrest adult consensual sex workers. This latest ruling deepens that framework by grounding it more firmly in constitutional rights of personal liberty and autonomy.

Why it matters

The judgment could curtail arbitrary police action against consensual adult sex workers and open the door to their accessing health, legal, and social services without fear of prosecution. It also sets a precedent for how courts weigh personal liberty against morality-based restrictions in Indian law.

What's next

Expect advocacy groups to use this ruling to push for legislative reform of the Immoral Traffic (Prevention) Act, while authorities will likely seek clearer implementation guidelines from the court.
