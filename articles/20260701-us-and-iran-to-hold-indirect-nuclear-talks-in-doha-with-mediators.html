US and Iran to Hold Indirect Nuclear Talks in Doha with Mediators

Key facts

  • US and Iran to hold indirect talks in Doha with mediators
  • Qatar is facilitating the backchannel diplomacy
  • Talks focus on Iran's nuclear programme
  • Outcome has direct implications for global oil prices

The United States and Iran are set to hold a fresh round of indirect negotiations in Doha, Qatar, with diplomats and mediators facilitating discussions between the two sides who do not engage in direct talks, a diplomat confirmed. The meeting is part of an ongoing, if fragile, diplomatic track aimed at addressing the impasse over Iran's nuclear programme.

Doha has become a preferred venue for such backchannel engagements, with Qatar's government playing an active mediating role between Washington and Tehran. The Gulf state has previously facilitated prisoner exchanges and other sensitive contacts between the two adversaries, making it a natural host for nuclear-adjacent diplomacy as well.

The talks come at a moment of considerable complexity. Iran's nuclear programme has advanced significantly since the collapse of the 2015 Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action, and the Trump administration — which in its first term tore up that deal — has signalled a willingness to negotiate a new arrangement while simultaneously maintaining maximum pressure through sanctions. Tehran, for its part, is under severe economic strain and has reason to seek relief, even as hardliners resist significant concessions.

For India, the stakes are tangible. India is a major importer of energy and has historically been a significant buyer of Iranian oil. A diplomatic breakthrough that eases sanctions on Iran could benefit Indian energy security and moderate global crude prices; a collapse of talks and further escalation would carry the opposite risk.
