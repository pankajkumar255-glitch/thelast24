Built by Her: How Women Are Changing India's Startup Story

What happened

The Times of India reports on the rising presence and influence of women founders within India's expanding startup ecosystem. The feature charts how women entrepreneurs are driving innovation and building significant businesses, contributing to the country's broader startup growth story. The piece comes against the backdrop of India's startup ecosystem reaching 2.3 lakh registered startups and generating millions of jobs. It draws attention to both the progress made and the structural challenges — including funding gaps and social barriers — that women founders continue to navigate.

The context

India's startup ecosystem has grown rapidly over the past decade, but women founders have historically received a disproportionately small share of venture capital funding globally and in India. Studies have consistently shown that all-women founding teams receive a fraction of total VC investment compared to all-male teams. However, initiatives like the government's Startup India programme, dedicated women entrepreneurship schemes, and a growing network of women-focused accelerators and angel investors have begun to shift the landscape. Prominent Indian women founders — such as those behind Nykaa, Mamaearth, and Sugar Cosmetics — have helped raise the visibility of women-led businesses.

Why it matters

As India's startup sector becomes a major engine of employment and innovation, ensuring women founders have equitable access to capital and support is critical to maximising that potential. Women-led startups often address underserved markets — including women consumers, rural communities, and healthcare — that male-dominated teams may overlook. Greater diversity in founding teams is also associated with better business outcomes and more resilient companies. For young Indian women, these success stories provide powerful role models and signal expanding professional possibilities.

What's next

Expect more policy attention and private sector initiatives focused on women entrepreneurship, particularly around access to early-stage funding and mentorship. The narrative around women founders is likely to gain further momentum as India's startup ecosystem matures and global investors increase scrutiny of diversity metrics.
