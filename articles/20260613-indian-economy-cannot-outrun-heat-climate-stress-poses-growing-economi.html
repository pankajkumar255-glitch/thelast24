Indian economy cannot outrun heat: climate stress poses growing economic threat

What happened

Hindustan Times reports that extreme heat is emerging as a binding constraint on India's economic potential, with the piece arguing the economy 'cannot outrun heat.' The report highlights the multi-channel damage heat inflicts — from agricultural productivity losses to reduced labour output in construction, manufacturing, and services. India has experienced several record-breaking heatwaves in recent years, and the economic literature increasingly quantifies the GDP cost of temperature rises. The report frames this as an urgent policy challenge rather than a distant environmental concern.

The context

India is one of the world's most heat-vulnerable major economies due to its large share of outdoor and informal workers, rain-fed agriculture, and rapidly urbanising population with limited cooling infrastructure. Studies by international bodies including the World Bank have estimated that South Asia could lose a significant share of working hours to heat stress by mid-century. The Indian government has introduced Heat Action Plans in several states, but implementation and funding remain uneven. Agriculture, which employs nearly 45 percent of India's workforce, is particularly exposed to temperature shocks that reduce crop yields and increase water stress.

Why it matters

Lost labour productivity directly translates into lower household incomes, particularly for daily wage workers in agriculture and construction who have no paid leave or compensation during heat shutdowns. Food inflation tends to spike after heat-damaged harvests, hitting the poorest consumers hardest. The economic cost of heat adaptation — cooling, urban redesign, crop insurance — will require substantial public and private investment that competes with other development priorities.

What's next

Expect heat stress to feature more prominently in India's budget allocations for climate adaptation and in RBI assessments of agricultural credit risk. Urban planners and state governments are likely to face growing pressure to mandate heat-resilient construction and expand green cover in cities.
