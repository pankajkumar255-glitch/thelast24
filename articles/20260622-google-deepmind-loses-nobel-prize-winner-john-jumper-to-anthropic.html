Google DeepMind Loses Nobel Prize Winner John Jumper to Anthropic

Key facts

  • John Jumper won the 2024 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for AlphaFold work
  • Jumper departs Google DeepMind for Anthropic
  • Anthropic is backed by Amazon, founded by ex-OpenAI researchers
  • AlphaFold predicted structures of virtually all known proteins

John Jumper, the Nobel Prize-winning scientist celebrated for cracking one of biology's hardest problems, has left Google DeepMind to join Anthropic, in one of the most high-profile talent moves the AI industry has seen in recent years. Jumper shared the 2024 Nobel Prize in Chemistry alongside DeepMind CEO Demis Hassabis for their work on AlphaFold, the AI system that predicted the three-dimensional structures of virtually all known proteins — a breakthrough that has accelerated drug discovery and biological research worldwide.

His move to Anthropic, the AI safety-focused company co-founded by former OpenAI researchers Dario and Daniela Amodei and backed by a major investment from Amazon, represents a striking talent acquisition for a firm that has positioned itself as a serious rival to both OpenAI and Google in the race to build frontier AI systems. Anthropic is the maker of the Claude family of AI models and has increasingly drawn top scientific and engineering talent from across the industry.

For DeepMind, losing Jumper is more than a symbolic setback. AlphaFold remains one of the most celebrated achievements in AI's history, and Jumper was its most prominent scientific face. The departure underscores the fierce competition among leading AI labs — OpenAI, Google DeepMind, Anthropic, and Meta AI — to recruit and retain the researchers who will define the next generation of AI capabilities.

The move also raises broader questions about the direction of AI research. Anthropic has staked its identity on safety-first AI development, and Jumper's arrival suggests the lab may be expanding its scientific ambitions beyond large language models into domains where his expertise in structural biology and deep learning could open new frontiers.
