1 in 3 lawyers in India fake, Supreme Court to examine digital registry for advocates

Key facts

  • Roughly 1 in 3 lawyers in India allegedly hold fake credentials
  • Supreme Court issued notice to Centre and Bar Council of India
  • Plea seeks a verified national digital registry of enrolled advocates
  • A social media code of conduct for lawyers also sought in the petition

The Times of India and The Hindu both report that the Supreme Court has issued notices to the Centre and the Bar Council of India (BCI) on a petition seeking a mandatory digital registry of all enrolled advocates and a social media code of conduct for the legal profession. The bench's intervention follows the startling assertion — accepted as serious enough for judicial examination — that approximately one in three lawyers in India may be operating on fraudulent credentials.

The plea argues that a centralised, publicly accessible digital registry would allow courts, litigants, and institutions to instantly verify whether a practising lawyer holds a genuine enrolment certificate issued by a State Bar Council. Currently, the verification system is fragmented across 25 state bar councils with little cross-referencing, making it relatively easy for impostors to present fake certificates and appear in courts or charge clients without detection.

The Bar Council of India and the Central government have been asked to file their responses. If the court directs the creation of such a registry, it would be one of the most significant reforms to the legal profession in decades — directly protecting millions of litigants across India who trust lawyers with their most urgent civil, criminal, and family matters.
