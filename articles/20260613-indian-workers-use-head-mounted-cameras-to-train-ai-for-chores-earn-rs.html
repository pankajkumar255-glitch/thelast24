Indian workers use head-mounted cameras to train AI for chores, earn Rs 250 hourly

What happened

India Today reports that Indian workers are being employed to wear head-mounted cameras while carrying out routine household tasks such as cooking and cleaning, with the footage used to train AI systems designed to understand and replicate physical human activity. Workers are compensated at a rate of Rs 250 per hour for this data-generation work. The arrangement is part of a broader global effort by AI companies to collect diverse, high-quality first-person video data of real-world tasks, which is essential for training AI models and robots intended to operate in domestic settings. The report highlights India as a significant source of this kind of human-labelled and human-generated training data.

The context

AI data labelling and annotation has been a growing source of employment in India for several years, with companies like Scale AI and others operating large data workforce programmes in the country. The collection of egocentric — or first-person — video data for training embodied AI and robotics is an emerging frontier, with projects like Meta's Ego4D having previously recruited participants globally including in India. As AI companies race to build robots and AI agents capable of performing physical tasks, the demand for diverse real-world domestic activity data has surged. India's large, cost-competitive workforce makes it an attractive source for this kind of data collection at scale.

Why it matters

For Indian workers, particularly those in lower-income brackets or in semi-urban and rural areas, Rs 250 per hour for relatively accessible work represents a meaningful income opportunity tied directly to the global AI economy. The trend points to a growing 'AI gig economy' in India that could provide livelihoods to thousands, though it also raises questions about data privacy, fair compensation, and the long-term sustainability of such roles as AI systems improve. For the broader Indian economy, this kind of data work positions India as a critical node in the global AI supply chain.

What's next

Expect more such data-collection programmes to expand across India as demand for physical task training data grows with advances in robotics and embodied AI. Regulatory and labour welfare questions around AI gig work are likely to come into focus as the scale of such employment grows.
