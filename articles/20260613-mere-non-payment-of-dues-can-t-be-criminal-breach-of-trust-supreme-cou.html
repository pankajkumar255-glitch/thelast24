Mere non-payment of dues can't be criminal breach of trust: Supreme Court

What happened

Deccan Herald reports that the Supreme Court has held that the mere non-payment of dues cannot be treated as criminal breach of trust under the Indian Penal Code. The court found that establishing criminal breach of trust requires demonstrating that the accused was entrusted with property or funds and then dishonestly misappropriated or converted them — a standard that unpaid dues alone do not meet. The ruling addresses a pattern of litigation in India where creditors, landlords, and business counterparties file criminal complaints under breach of trust provisions as leverage in what are fundamentally civil recovery matters. By restricting this practice, the court reinforces the distinction between civil liability and criminal culpability.

The context

Section 405 of the Indian Penal Code (now mirrored in the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita) defines criminal breach of trust and has been the subject of significant judicial interpretation over the decades. Courts have repeatedly noted that the provision is frequently misused to convert civil disputes into criminal cases, with complainants using the threat of arrest and prosecution to force settlement. The Supreme Court and various high courts have in the past quashed criminal proceedings initiated on similar grounds, holding that dishonest intent — not mere non-payment — is the crux of the offence. This latest ruling adds to that body of precedent and is likely to be widely cited in trial courts and high courts.

Why it matters

For businesses, traders, landlords, and individuals involved in financial disputes, the ruling provides some protection against the weaponisation of criminal law in what are civil matters. It also reduces the burden on criminal courts, which are already severely overburdened, by discouraging the filing of frivolous criminal complaints. At the same time, genuine victims of fraudulent misappropriation retain their legal recourse, as the ruling does not dilute the offence itself but clarifies its essential ingredients. The decision contributes to a clearer and fairer separation between India's civil and criminal justice systems.

What's next

Expect trial courts and high courts to cite this ruling when hearing applications to quash criminal breach of trust complaints filed in commercial disputes. Lawyers advising clients in recovery matters will need to reassess whether criminal complaints under breach of trust provisions are viable strategies. Further clarification from the Supreme Court on the boundaries between civil and criminal liability in financial matters may follow as related cases come up.
