The Philosopher Inside Google DeepMind Wrestling With What AI Actually Is

Key facts

  • Google DeepMind employs an in-house philosopher to study the nature of AI
  • Even DeepMind's own researchers describe AI's nature as a 'deep mystery'
  • Questions of AI consciousness and moral status are under active internal discussion

Inside Google DeepMind, one of the world's foremost artificial intelligence research laboratories, a philosopher is asking the questions that the engineers cannot answer: what, actually, is this thing we are building? The Guardian's profile of DeepMind's resident philosopher lays bare a striking admission at the heart of the global AI boom — that even those creating the most advanced AI systems are deeply uncertain about the nature of what they have made.

The 'deep mystery', as the philosopher describes it, is not merely academic. It has direct implications for how AI should be governed, what rights or moral status AI systems might one day claim, and how society should prepare for technologies whose inner workings remain opaque even to their creators. DeepMind, which is owned by Alphabet — Google's parent company — has been at the centre of landmark AI breakthroughs, from AlphaGo to AlphaFold, making the philosophical uncertainty all the more consequential.

The profile touches on questions of consciousness, agency, and moral responsibility — themes that are moving rapidly from science fiction into boardrooms and legislatures. As AI systems become more capable and more embedded in daily life, the inability to clearly define what they are makes questions of accountability and safety considerably harder to resolve.

For India, which is developing its own national AI framework and seeing rapid deployment of AI in sectors from agriculture to healthcare, these foundational uncertainties matter enormously. Policy built on the assumption that AI is simply a sophisticated tool may be wholly inadequate if the technology turns out to be something more — or something altogether different. The philosopher's questions, in that sense, are ones that Indian policymakers and citizens cannot afford to ignore.
