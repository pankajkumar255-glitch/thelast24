Supreme Court rewrites India's view on sex work, consent and personal liberty

What happened

The Times of India reports that the Supreme Court has delivered a significant ruling that changes the legal framework around sex work and consent in India. The court appears to have grounded its judgment in the constitutional right to personal liberty, marking a departure from earlier morality-based approaches. The ruling addresses how adult consensual decisions must be viewed by the state and the judiciary. This comes alongside a separate Supreme Court observation that consensual pre-marital relationships are not a mark on a person's character. The Hindu similarly reports the court stating that 'times have changed' and that pre-marital physical relationships do not constitute moral turpitude.

The context

India's legal system has historically treated sex work through the Immoral Traffic (Prevention) Act, which criminalises aspects of the trade while leaving sex workers in a legal grey zone. The Supreme Court has in previous years directed states to treat sex workers with dignity and not arrest them for their profession. Personal liberty jurisprudence in India has expanded significantly since the 2017 Puttaswamy judgment, which recognised the right to privacy as a fundamental right. Social and moral frameworks around consensual adult relationships have long been contested in Indian courts, with rulings on live-in relationships and pre-marital sex evolving over decades.

Why it matters

For sex workers and marginalised communities across India, this ruling could offer greater legal protection from arbitrary police action and social stigma. The emphasis on consent and liberty over morality sets a judicial precedent that could influence future rulings on personal freedoms. Families, couples, and individuals whose private choices were previously judged by courts as morally deficient now have stronger legal standing. The ruling also signals that India's highest court is increasingly aligning personal freedom with constitutional rights rather than traditional social norms.

What's next

Expect advocacy groups and legal experts to study the ruling closely for its precise scope and how lower courts interpret and apply it. State governments and law enforcement agencies will likely need to revise guidelines on how they engage with sex workers and consensual adult relationships.
