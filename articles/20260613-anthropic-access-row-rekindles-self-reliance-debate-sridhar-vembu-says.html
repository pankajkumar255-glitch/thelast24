Anthropic access row rekindles self-reliance debate; Sridhar Vembu says 'Globalisation Is Dead'

What happened

Deccan Herald reports that the US government's ban on Indians and other foreign nationals accessing Anthropic's Claude Fable 5 and Mythos AI models has reignited a debate about India's technological self-reliance. India Today explains the ban is rooted in US government export control directives that restrict the availability of frontier AI models to non-US users. Zoho founder and technology commentator Sridhar Vembu has responded by declaring that globalisation is effectively dead, arguing that India must urgently build indigenous AI capabilities rather than depend on American platforms. The access restriction has created practical disruption for Indian developers, researchers, and businesses that relied on Anthropic's advanced models.

The context

Anthropic is one of the leading US-based AI companies, known for its Claude series of large language models, which are widely used by developers and enterprises globally. US export control laws — including the Export Administration Regulations — have increasingly been applied to advanced AI technologies, particularly those with potential dual-use military applications. India, despite being a US strategic partner, is not exempt from these restrictions when they are applied broadly to all non-US persons. Sridhar Vembu of Zoho has been one of India's most prominent advocates for technology sovereignty, having built Zoho as a bootstrapped, India-headquartered alternative to Silicon Valley software giants. The episode echoes earlier tensions over US chip export controls that affected India's semiconductor ambitions.

Why it matters

For Indian companies and startups that have built products or workflows on top of Anthropic's Claude models, the access ban creates immediate operational disruption and forces costly pivots to alternative platforms. More broadly, it validates the concerns of technology sovereignty advocates who have long argued that India's digital infrastructure must not depend on foreign platforms that can be switched off by geopolitical decisions in Washington. The timing — coinciding with the launch of India's own Varya video AI model — makes the case for the India AI Mission's agenda more compelling to policymakers and the private sector alike.

What's next

Expect Indian AI startups and enterprises to accelerate migration to domestic or neutral AI platforms following the Anthropic access shock. Watch for the government to use this episode to fast-track India AI Mission deployments and encourage private sector investment in indigenous models. Sridhar Vembu and other technology voices are likely to intensify their advocacy for policy measures that mandate or incentivise the use of Indian-built AI infrastructure.
