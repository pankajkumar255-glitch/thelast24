Anthropic access row rekindles self-reliance debate; Sridhar Vembu says 'Globalisation Is Dead'

What happened

Deccan Herald reports that the US government's order to disable Anthropic's Fable 5 and Mythos models for foreign users has rekindled India's self-reliance debate in the technology sector. Sridhar Vembu, founder of Zoho and a long-standing advocate for Indian software independence, responded to the development with the pointed remark that 'Globalisation Is Dead.' The Anthropic access row is being seen as a real-world demonstration of the risks of building critical business and research infrastructure on foreign AI platforms that are subject to US government control. The episode has brought together voices across the Indian tech ecosystem calling for accelerated investment in domestic AI models and infrastructure.

The context

Sridhar Vembu has for years been a vocal proponent of Indian technological self-sufficiency, having built Zoho into a global software company headquartered in rural Tamil Nadu. India has made notable investments in AI through government initiatives such as IndiaAI Mission, which aims to fund computing infrastructure and homegrown model development. The broader global debate around AI sovereignty — who controls the models, the data, and the compute — has intensified as the United States and China compete for dominance in frontier AI. India has repeatedly found itself navigating between these two poles, seeking access to advanced technology while also pursuing indigenous capability.

Why it matters

The debate triggered by this episode could shape India's AI policy direction for years, potentially accelerating public and private investment in domestic AI models and reducing reliance on foreign platforms. For Indian businesses and developers, it underscores the risk of building products and services on infrastructure controlled by foreign governments. If India succeeds in developing credible sovereign AI alternatives, it could also open new export opportunities and strengthen the country's strategic autonomy in a technology-defined world.

What's next

Expect louder calls in Parliament and industry forums for a dedicated push toward Indian large language models and sovereign AI compute. Government programmes like the IndiaAI Mission are likely to see renewed urgency and potentially increased funding commitments.
