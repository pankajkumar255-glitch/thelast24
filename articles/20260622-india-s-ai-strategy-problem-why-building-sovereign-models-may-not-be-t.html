India's AI strategy problem: Why building sovereign models may not be the answer

Key facts

  • India's IndiaAI Mission is funding sovereign AI model development
  • Analysis argues sovereign models are too costly and slow for India
  • Alternative: focus on AI applications, data infrastructure, regulation
  • Frontier AI model training costs tens of billions of dollars globally

India's ambition to build sovereign artificial intelligence models — large-scale foundation systems developed and controlled domestically — is drawing serious pushback from policy analysts, with a prominent Business Standard opinion piece arguing that the strategy may be fundamentally misconceived.

The core of the critique is economic and practical. Training frontier AI models requires tens of billions of dollars, enormous GPU clusters, and years of sustained effort — resources that even well-funded Western and Chinese labs strain to deploy. For India, which is still building out the compute infrastructure backbone through the IndiaAI Mission, attempting to match the likes of GPT-4 or Gemini on a state budget risks enormous expenditure for systems that could be obsolete before deployment.

Critics of the sovereign model push argue India's genuine advantages lie elsewhere: in its vast and linguistically diverse data, its large pool of AI engineers, and its unique position as a high-volume user market that can shape how global AI systems are fine-tuned and adapted. A strategy centred on application layers, domain-specific models for healthcare, agriculture, and governance, and strong data-governance laws could yield far greater returns than frontier model development.

The debate is live and consequential. The government's IndiaAI Mission has already committed thousands of crores toward GPU capacity and model development. Whether that investment is best directed at building sovereign foundation models or at enabling Indian developers to build on top of global ones will define the country's AI trajectory for the next decade — and the Business Standard piece suggests the current strategy needs a harder look.
