India Built a Startup Ecosystem. Now It Needs an Innovation Ecosystem

Key facts

  • India has 100+ unicorns but remains a net technology importer
  • Indian R&D spending as GDP share lags peer economies
  • Indian startups largely built on foreign chips, cloud, and AI models
  • YourStory calls for university-industry-government alignment on innovation

India's startup ecosystem is one of the world's largest by deal count and valuation, home to over a hundred unicorns and a generation of founders who have reshaped how a billion people bank, shop, learn, and move. But a sharp new analysis published by YourStory argues that this achievement, impressive as it is, masks a more troubling gap: India has not yet built an innovation ecosystem.

The distinction matters. A startup ecosystem excels at applying existing technology to new markets — and India has done this brilliantly, from UPI-powered fintech to hyperlocal logistics. An innovation ecosystem, by contrast, generates the underlying technology itself: the chips, the algorithms, the materials, the drugs, the scientific breakthroughs that startups everywhere else then commercialise. On that measure, India remains a net importer.

The YourStory piece points to the structural reasons: Indian academia has historically been disconnected from industry, R&D investment as a share of GDP remains well below that of peer economies, and venture capital has rationally chased shorter-horizon returns in consumer sectors. The result is that India's most celebrated companies are largely built on foreign cloud infrastructure, foreign chips, and foreign foundational models.

Changing this requires more than policy statements. It demands patient capital, reformed university incentives, and a culture that rewards long-term scientific risk-taking alongside the sprint-to-market ethos that has characterised the startup generation. The question is whether India's ecosystem — investors, founders, institutions, and government — is ready to make that shift before the window narrows.
