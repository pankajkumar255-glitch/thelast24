Supreme Court reviews Bihar's repeated reappointment of unelected minister

What happened

India Today NE reports that the Supreme Court is reviewing the Bihar government's reappointment of a minister who has not secured election to the state legislature, citing Ambedkar's cautions from the Constituent Assembly about such practices. The court's scrutiny suggests concern that the constitutional six-month grace period for non-elected ministers is being misused through repeated reappointments.

The context

The Indian Constitution under Article 164(4) allows a non-legislator to serve as a minister for a maximum of six consecutive months, after which they must win a seat or vacate office. In practice, some state governments have circumvented this by rotating ministerial appointments or reappointing individuals after brief gaps. Ambedkar had flagged during the Constituent Assembly debates that such practices could undermine democratic accountability.

Why it matters

A ruling against such reappointments would strengthen democratic accountability across all Indian states, ensuring that ministerial power is held by persons who have faced voters — a foundational principle of representative government.

What's next

Expect the Bihar government to file its detailed response before the next hearing, with constitutional law experts watching closely for a ruling that could set binding precedent for all state governments.
