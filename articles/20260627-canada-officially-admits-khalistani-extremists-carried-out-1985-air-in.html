Canada Officially Admits Khalistani Extremists Carried Out 1985 Air India Kanishka Bombing

Key facts

  • Canada admits Khalistani extremists carried out 1985 Air India Kanishka bombing
  • 329 people killed in the attack — Canada's deadliest terror incident
  • Canada calls it a 'heinous act of terror'
  • Admission comes 40 years after the bombing
  • Follows prolonged India-Canada diplomatic tensions over Khalistani extremism

Forty years after one of aviation history's deadliest atrocities, Canada has officially admitted that Khalistani extremists carried out the 1985 bombing of Air India Flight 182, the Kanishka, which killed all 329 people aboard. The Canadian government called it a 'heinous act of terror', marking the first unambiguous official acknowledgement of the perpetrators' ideological identity.

The Kanishka was brought down on 23 June 1985 as it flew from Montreal to London en route to Delhi. The majority of victims were Canadian citizens of Indian origin, and the bombing had long been attributed to Sikh separatist groups operating out of Canada, though official Canadian statements had historically been more circumspect about labelling the act as Khalistani terrorism.

The admission comes at a diplomatically charged moment. India and Canada have endured a prolonged and bitter dispute over Canada's handling of Khalistani separatist activities on its soil, including the fallout from the killing of Hardeep Singh Nijjar. New Delhi has consistently demanded that Ottawa do more to rein in extremist elements, and this acknowledgement — however belated — represents a meaningful shift in Canada's public posture.

For survivors, victims' families, and the Indian government, the statement is a long-overdue recognition of the truth. Whether it translates into tangible policy consequences for Khalistani groups operating in Canada will be the question that defines its lasting significance.
