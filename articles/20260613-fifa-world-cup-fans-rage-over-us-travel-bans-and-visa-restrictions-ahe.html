FIFA World Cup Fans Rage Over US Travel Bans and Visa Restrictions Ahead of 2026 Tournament

What happened

BBC reports that football fans are expressing significant anger over US travel bans and visa restrictions that could bar supporters from several nations from attending the 2026 FIFA World Cup. The tournament is scheduled to be held across the United States, Canada, and Mexico. Fans quoted in the report describe the situation as 'a World Cup for them not us', highlighting the exclusionary impact of current US immigration and travel policies under the Trump administration. The story was published at 04:30 IST.

The context

The 2026 FIFA World Cup will be the first to feature 48 teams and is expected to draw record crowds across North American host cities. The United States is hosting the bulk of matches, with venues across cities including New York, Los Angeles, Dallas, and Miami. The Trump administration has implemented broad travel restrictions and enhanced visa scrutiny targeting nationals from several countries since January 2025. FIFA awarded the tournament to the US-Canada-Mexico bid in 2018, and access and visa facilitation were key commitments made during the bidding process. Previous World Cups have seen fans from across the globe travel freely to host nations.

Why it matters

Indian football fans and members of the large Indian diaspora in the US who want to attend matches could face heightened visa scrutiny or outright denial of entry, complicating travel plans. The controversy also puts FIFA under pressure to enforce access guarantees it secured from host nations, setting a precedent for future tournaments. More broadly, it reflects how Trump-era immigration policies are having real cultural and sporting consequences beyond politics, affecting ordinary people's ability to participate in global events.

What's next

Expect FIFA to face mounting pressure from national football associations and fan groups to seek formal visa guarantees from the US government ahead of the tournament. If restrictions are not eased, some countries may formally raise the issue through diplomatic channels in the months leading up to the June 2026 kickoff.
