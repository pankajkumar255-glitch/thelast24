India Can Handle Oil at $90 a Barrel, but Risks Mount Beyond That: Avendus

What happened

Moneycontrol reports that Avendus, a prominent Indian investment firm, has stated that India can absorb crude oil prices at the $90-per-barrel level, but that growth and inflation risks escalate significantly if prices rise beyond that point. The assessment appears to be part of a broader macroeconomic analysis by the firm. The report sets a clear price threshold above which India's fiscal position, current account deficit, and retail inflation would come under compounding pressure. No specific GDP or inflation figures were cited in the headline, but the directional warning is clear.

The context

India is the world's third-largest oil importer, meeting roughly 85% of its crude oil needs through imports. This makes the economy structurally sensitive to global oil price swings. When crude prices rise sharply, the government faces a difficult choice between passing costs on to consumers — fuelling inflation — or absorbing them through subsidies, which strains fiscal accounts. The RBI also watches oil prices closely, as they feed directly into the Consumer Price Index. India has in the past benefited from discounted Russian crude following Western sanctions, which partially buffered price shocks.

Why it matters

If oil crosses and stays above $90 per barrel, expect petrol and diesel prices at the pump to rise, pushing up transport and logistics costs across the economy. Higher fuel prices also raise input costs for farmers, manufacturers, and food delivery, ultimately hitting household budgets. The government may be forced to choose between widening the fiscal deficit or allowing retail price hikes ahead of any election cycle. RBI's room to cut interest rates would also narrow if inflation picks up on the back of oil.

What's next

Expect the RBI and Finance Ministry to closely monitor Brent crude movements relative to the $90 threshold Avendus identifies. Any sustained breach of that level will likely trigger fresh debate about fuel subsidy policy and prompt revisions to India's growth forecasts for the year.
