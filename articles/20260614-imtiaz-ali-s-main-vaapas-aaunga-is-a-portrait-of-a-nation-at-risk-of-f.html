Imtiaz Ali's 'Main Vaapas Aaunga' Is a Portrait of a Nation at Risk of Forgetting Itself

What happened

The Indian Express has published a review of Imtiaz Ali's Main Vaapas Aaunga, describing it as a film that examines a country in danger of losing its sense of self. The review suggests the film is a significant and thematically ambitious work from the director. The headline frames the film's central concern as the erosion of memory and identity at a societal level. The Indian Express is among India's most respected film criticism outlets, lending the review considerable weight. Details about the cast, release platform, or box-office performance were not specified in the headline.

The context

Imtiaz Ali is one of contemporary Hindi cinema's most celebrated auteurs, known for films such as Jab We Met, Rockstar, Highway, and Tamasha, which consistently explore themes of personal identity, longing, and the search for meaning. His films have often used journeys — both literal and emotional — as metaphors for self-discovery. Ali's work has a devoted following among audiences who appreciate literary and introspective cinema, even when his films divide mainstream critics. Main Vaapas Aaunga, whose title translates roughly to 'I Will Come Back', suggests themes of return, nostalgia, and belonging that align with his established thematic preoccupations. The film arrives at a time of intense national debate about heritage, culture, and modernity in India.

Why it matters

A film by a director of Imtiaz Ali's stature that directly engages with questions of Indian cultural identity carries significance beyond entertainment, entering ongoing public conversations about what India is and where it is headed. For audiences, it offers rare mainstream cinema that grapples with ideas rather than spectacle. Critical attention from publications like The Indian Express can shape the film's reception and longevity in public discourse.

What's next

Expect wider critical and audience response to emerge as the film reaches more viewers, and likely debate about its cultural and political readings. The film's commercial performance will also test whether introspective, identity-driven cinema continues to find an audience in post-pandemic India.
