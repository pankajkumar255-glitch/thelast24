Supreme Court dismisses businessman's plea against ED searches in TASMAC liquor case

Key facts

  • Supreme Court dismissed plea challenging ED searches in TASMAC case
  • TASMAC is Tamil Nadu's state-run liquor monopoly corporation
  • ED is probing money-laundering allegations in TASMAC's supply chain
  • Dismissal clears ED to continue investigation without judicial interference

The Supreme Court has dismissed a petition filed by a businessman challenging the Enforcement Directorate's search operations in the TASMAC case, refusing to interfere with the federal agency's ongoing money-laundering investigation. The bench junked the plea, effectively endorsing the ED's authority to conduct searches in connection with alleged financial irregularities at the Tamil Nadu State Marketing Corporation, better known as TASMAC.

TASMAC, which holds a monopoly over liquor retail in Tamil Nadu, has been at the centre of a prolonged ED investigation into allegations of kickbacks, irregular procurement, and money laundering in its supply chain. The case carries significant political weight, given that TASMAC is a major revenue generator for the state and its operations touch thousands of employees and licensed vendors.

By declining to entertain the businessman's challenge to the searches, the Supreme Court has reinforced the ED's broad powers under the Prevention of Money Laundering Act to conduct raids and searches during investigations. Petitioners in such cases often argue that searches are conducted without adequate legal basis, but courts have repeatedly upheld PMLA's expansive investigative framework.

The ruling, as reported by The New Indian Express, means the ED faces no judicial obstacle to continuing its probe. For the Tamil Nadu government and those under the agency's scanner, the dismissal is a significant legal setback, and further action — including arrests or charge sheets — could follow as the investigation progresses.
