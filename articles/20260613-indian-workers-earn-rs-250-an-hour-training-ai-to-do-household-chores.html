Indian workers earn Rs 250 an hour training AI to do household chores using head-mounted cameras

What happened

India Today reports that Indian workers are participating in AI training programmes where they wear head-mounted cameras to record household activities such as cooking and cleaning, earning around Rs 250 per hour. The data collected is used to train AI models designed to understand and eventually replicate domestic tasks. This is part of the broader global AI data labelling and training industry, in which India has become a significant hub due to its large, English-literate, and relatively low-cost workforce. The head-mounted camera format is specifically designed to capture first-person perspective data that is essential for training embodied AI and robotics systems.

The context

India is already one of the world's largest markets for AI data annotation and labelling work, with hundreds of thousands of workers employed by companies ranging from large BPOs to informal gig platforms. Global AI labs and robotics companies require enormous volumes of human-generated demonstration data to train AI systems on physical tasks — a field called imitation learning or behaviour cloning. The work described here is particularly relevant to the emerging field of household robotics and AI assistants, where companies like Google, Amazon, and several well-funded startups are racing to build systems that can perform domestic tasks autonomously. Rs 250 per hour is above India's average daily wage for informal labour, making it an attractive option.

Why it matters

This kind of work creates a new income stream for Indian households, particularly those in semi-urban and rural areas who can participate from home with minimal equipment. However, it also raises questions about data privacy, since workers are recording detailed video of their homes and daily routines for foreign AI companies. The broader pattern — India providing cheap human labour to train AI systems that may eventually displace service workers globally — is an economic and policy tension worth watching.

What's next

Expect this segment of AI data work to grow rapidly in India as robotics and embodied AI research accelerates globally, and watch for regulatory attention on privacy standards around home-recorded training data.
