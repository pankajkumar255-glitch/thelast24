Marco Rubio To Visit Gulf Countries As Hormuz Tensions Stay In Focus

Key facts

  • Rubio begins Gulf tour Thursday amid US-Iran nuclear talks
  • Strait of Hormuz — through which ~20% of global oil passes — in focus
  • Gulf states anxious about any US-Iran rapprochement
  • Tour follows JD Vance's hint at imminent Iran nuclear deal

US Secretary of State Marco Rubio is embarking on a tour of Gulf countries beginning Thursday, with the Strait of Hormuz — through which a fifth of the world's oil supply flows — the central security concern shaping his agenda.

The visit comes at a pivotal moment in US-Iran diplomacy. Hours before Rubio's departure, Vice President JD Vance publicly hinted at a potential nuclear deal with Tehran, including the possible unfreezing of Iranian assets. Gulf states, many of which view Iran with deep suspicion, will be anxious to understand Washington's red lines and what security guarantees are on offer should a deal be struck.

Saudi Arabia, the UAE, Qatar and other Gulf Cooperation Council members have long-standing concerns about Iranian influence in the region, including Tehran's capacity to threaten or disrupt Hormuz shipping. Any American diplomatic engagement with Iran requires intensive parallel outreach to these allies to prevent a rupture in relationships that underpin both US security architecture in the region and global energy markets.

For India, which imports the bulk of its crude oil through Gulf shipping lanes and has significant diaspora and trade interests across the region, the outcome of Rubio's tour carries real economic consequences. Stability — or instability — in Hormuz has a direct and near-immediate effect on crude prices that eventually reach Indian consumers at the pump.
