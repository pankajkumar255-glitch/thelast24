What Made the Supreme Court Put a Monetary Value on a Homemaker's Labour

What happened

The Hindu examines what prompted the Supreme Court to quantify homemakers' labour, situating it in the context of motor accident compensation jurisprudence and broader questions of gender equity in law. The court's reasoning engages with the long-standing invisibility of unpaid domestic work in economic and legal accounting, and establishes a framework for assigning notional income to those who perform it.

The context

Indian courts have historically struggled to assign compensation for the death or disability of homemakers in accident cases, often awarding token amounts since homemakers do not draw a formal salary. Several High Courts had adopted varying notional income figures, creating inconsistency. The Supreme Court has now stepped in to standardise this, drawing on internationally recognised principles that domestic labour — cooking, childcare, elder care — constitutes real economic output.

Why it matters

Standardised valuation of homemakers' labour will directly raise compensation payouts in accident and insurance cases, benefiting millions of families across India, particularly in lower-income households where women do not hold formal employment.

What's next

Expect insurance companies, motor accident claims tribunals, and lower courts to revise their compensation calculations in line with the Supreme Court's new framework.
