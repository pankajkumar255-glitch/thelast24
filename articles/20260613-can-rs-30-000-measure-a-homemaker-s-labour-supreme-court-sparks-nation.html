Can Rs 30,000 measure a homemaker's labour? Supreme Court sparks national debate

What happened

India Today reports that the Supreme Court has issued a ruling quantifying a homemaker's labour at Rs 30,000 per month for the purposes of legal compensation. The story centres on the reactions of women across India who are questioning whether this figure is adequate or even a meaningful measure of unpaid domestic work. The ruling appears to arise in the context of compensation claims — likely motor accident or wrongful death cases — where courts must assign an economic value to the deceased or injured homemaker's contributions. The judgment marks a formal judicial acknowledgment that homemaking constitutes real, compensable economic labour.

The context

Indian courts have long struggled with how to value the unpaid labour of homemakers, particularly in motor accident compensation cases adjudicated under the Motor Vehicles Act. Historically, courts either awarded minimal or no compensation for homemakers on the grounds that they earned no formal income. Earlier Supreme Court judgments had nudged towards recognising domestic work's economic value, but a concrete monthly figure had remained elusive. Women constitute the overwhelming majority of full-time homemakers in India, and their labour — cooking, childcare, eldercare, household management — has been estimated by economists to account for a significant share of GDP if formally counted. The ruling builds on a broader global conversation about unpaid care work and its place in national accounting.

Why it matters

For families that lose a homemaker to accident or negligence, this ruling could meaningfully raise the compensation they receive in court. Insurance companies and tribunals will now have a clearer, if debated, benchmark to apply. However, critics argue Rs 30,000 per month undervalues the actual cost of replacing domestic services in urban India, where hiring help for cooking, cleaning, and childcare can easily exceed this figure. The ruling also carries symbolic weight — it sends a signal that the legal system views homemaking as work deserving recognition, which could influence future legislation on social security and pension rights for homemakers.

What's next

Expect legal advocates and women's rights groups to debate whether Rs 30,000 is a floor or a ceiling, and whether it should be indexed to inflation or regional cost of living. Future cases will likely test whether this figure can be revised upward, and Parliament may face pressure to codify homemaker rights more broadly in social security law.
