What Is the 'Period Tax' Pakistan Is Moving to Scrap?

Key facts

  • Pakistan is moving to scrap GST on menstrual hygiene products
  • Products currently taxed as non-essential consumer goods
  • UK abolished its period tax in 2021; 30+ countries have followed
  • India exempted basic sanitary napkins from GST in 2018, but debate continues

The Indian Express explains the 'period tax' — a sales or goods-and-services tax applied to menstrual hygiene products — as Pakistan's parliament moves to scrap it. In Pakistan, these products have been taxed as non-essential consumer goods, making them less accessible for lower-income women. The proposed repeal would categorise sanitary pads and similar products as essential health items, exempting them from GST.

The 'period tax' debate is not new — the UK abolished it in 2021, and over 30 countries have now either zero-rated or fully exempted menstrual products from sales tax. Advocates argue that taxing these products as luxuries discriminates against women and worsens period poverty, particularly in low-income communities.

In India, the issue is directly relevant. While basic sanitary napkins were exempted from GST in 2018 following sustained campaigning, a range of menstrual products still attract taxation. Pakistan's move is likely to reinvigorate civil society pressure on the Indian government to go further, extending relief across all menstrual hygiene products.
