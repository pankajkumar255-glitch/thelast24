How India's economy weathered the West Asia shock

Key facts

  • India imports over 85% of its crude oil, largely from Gulf states
  • Gulf diaspora remittances form India's largest single source of inward remittances
  • India had diversified some crude sourcing to discounted Russian oil post-Ukraine war
  • Red Sea and Hormuz Strait shipping disruptions raised freight costs for Indian trade

The Indian Express examines how India's economy managed to navigate the disruption triggered by the West Asia conflict without suffering severe macroeconomic damage. The conflict raised alarm bells in New Delhi given India's deep economic ties to the region — the country sources the bulk of its crude oil from Gulf states and depends on the Gulf diaspora for a significant share of its annual remittance inflows.

Several structural buffers appear to have helped India weather the shock. India had already diversified a portion of its crude sourcing toward discounted Russian oil following the Ukraine conflict, which provided a partial cushion against Gulf supply disruptions. Robust foreign exchange reserves also gave the Reserve Bank of India room to manage currency volatility without drastic intervention.

Shipping disruptions through the Red Sea and Strait of Hormuz added to logistics costs across the economy, with Indian exporters and importers absorbing higher freight rates. However, a relatively contained duration of the most acute phase of the conflict, combined with diplomatic balancing by New Delhi, prevented a worst-case scenario.

The episode underlines India's continued vulnerability to West Asian instability even as it builds resilience — a reminder that energy security and the welfare of the nearly nine million Indians working in the Gulf remain central concerns for economic policymakers.
