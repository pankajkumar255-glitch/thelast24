Pre-marital consensual relationships no blot on character, rules Supreme Court

What happened

The Hindu and NDTV report that the Supreme Court has explicitly stated that consensual pre-marital relationships cannot be treated as a reflection of poor character. The court's observation is particularly significant in the context of criminal cases, where defence lawyers have often used a woman's prior relationships to undermine her credibility or question her moral standing. The judgment reinforces the principle that consent is the defining factor in assessing relationships, not marital status. The ruling is consistent with recent Supreme Court jurisprudence expanding the scope of personal liberty and individual autonomy under Article 21 of the Constitution.

The context

Indian law and society have long attached stigma to pre-marital relationships, particularly for women. In rape and sexual assault trials, defence arguments frequently attempted to use a survivor's past consensual relationships to suggest consent or to impugn her character — a practice sometimes referred to as 'slut-shaming' in legal discourse. Amendments to the Indian Evidence Act have progressively curtailed the ability of defence counsel to introduce a survivor's sexual history as evidence, but judicial attitudes have not always kept pace with legislative intent. The Supreme Court has in recent years delivered a series of judgments expanding the right to privacy, bodily autonomy, and personal liberty, including the landmark Puttaswamy privacy judgment and rulings on the rights of LGBTQ+ individuals.

Why it matters

This ruling is likely to have immediate impact in courtrooms across India, where trial courts will be reminded that a person's consensual pre-marital relationship history cannot be weaponised to question their character or credibility. For women in particular, it offers a measure of protection against a form of bias that has undermined justice in countless cases. The broader cultural message — that the state and the judiciary will not sit in moral judgment over consensual adult relationships — is significant in a society where such stigma causes real harm. It may also influence how police, prosecutors, and lower court judges approach evidence and witness credibility.

What's next

Expect defence lawyers in criminal cases to face greater judicial resistance when attempting to introduce a complainant's pre-marital relationship history. Lower courts will likely be directed or reminded by High Courts to apply this principle in pending cases. Legal reformers may push for this judicial position to be codified in updated evidence and criminal procedure laws.
