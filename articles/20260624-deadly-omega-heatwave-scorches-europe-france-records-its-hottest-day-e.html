Deadly 'Omega' heatwave scorches Europe, France records its hottest day ever with 40 drownings

Key facts

  • France recorded its hottest day ever during the 'Omega' heatwave
  • 40 people drowned across France seeking relief from heat
  • More temperature records expected as heatwave persists
  • France reported first major power outage linked to the heatwave

A deadly weather system Europeans are calling the 'Omega' heatwave has swept across the continent, breaking temperature records and killing dozens as governments struggle to protect vulnerable populations. France bore some of the worst of it, recording its hottest day ever and reporting 40 drownings across the country — a tragically predictable consequence of people flocking to unguarded waterways to escape the heat.

The heatwave takes its name from the Omega blocking pattern in the atmosphere — a high-pressure system that parks itself over a region and deflects cooler air, trapping searing temperatures beneath it. Meteorologists warned that more records are expected before the system breaks, with southern and central Europe most severely affected.

BBC footage showed Europeans lining up at municipal cool-down spots, while some residents were seen chalking windows to reflect sunlight — improvised responses to an infrastructure not built for sustained extreme heat. France also reported its first major power outage linked to the heatwave, adding a dangerous layer of vulnerability for elderly and ill residents who rely on fans and air conditioning.

The Omega heatwave arrives as climate scientists have repeatedly warned that extreme heat events are becoming more frequent, more intense, and longer-lasting across the northern hemisphere. For a watching world, the European crisis is a preview of what unmitigated warming looks like at scale.
