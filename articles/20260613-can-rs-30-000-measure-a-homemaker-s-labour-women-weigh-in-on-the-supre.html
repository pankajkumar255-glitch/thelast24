Can Rs 30,000 measure a homemaker's labour? Women weigh in on the Supreme Court ruling

What happened

India Today reports that following the Supreme Court's landmark ruling valuing homemakers' work at a minimum of Rs 30,000 per month, women from various backgrounds have shared their views on whether the benchmark is sufficient or symbolic. Some respondents welcomed the formal recognition of domestic work as economically meaningful, while others questioned whether Rs 30,000 — roughly the wage of an entry-level urban worker — truly reflects the round-the-clock nature of homemaking. The reactions highlight the complexity of assigning a monetary value to labour that is inherently relational and context-dependent. The story adds a crucial ground-level dimension to what is otherwise a legal and policy development.

The context

The question of how to value unpaid domestic work has been debated by economists, feminists, and policymakers for decades. India's GDP does not account for household production, a gap that feminist economists have long argued renders women's contributions invisible in national accounting. Time-use surveys conducted by the government have shown that Indian women spend significantly more hours per day on unpaid care and domestic work than men. The Supreme Court's intervention in this space, while limited to compensation in legal cases, is seen as part of a slow but meaningful shift in institutional attitudes. International frameworks such as the UN's Sustainable Development Goals also call for the recognition and valuation of unpaid care work.

Why it matters

The public response captured by India Today illustrates that the Supreme Court's ruling resonates far beyond courtrooms — it touches the daily reality of crores of Indian women who manage households without pay or formal recognition. If the Rs 30,000 benchmark is seen as too low, it could prompt further legal challenges or legislative action to revise it upward. The conversation also has implications for how families negotiate domestic responsibilities and how women's contributions are acknowledged within households. Over time, such rulings can shift cultural norms as much as legal ones.

What's next

Expect women's rights organisations and legal advocates to analyse whether Rs 30,000 should be treated as a floor that rises with inflation or cost of living. Further petitions or public interest litigation may seek to extend the ruling's logic beyond accident compensation to other areas of family law. The debate is likely to continue in media and civil society as the ruling's practical applications become clearer.
