India launches world's first hydrogen production facility using nuclear technology

Key facts

  • Described as the world's first hydrogen facility using nuclear technology
  • Positions India ahead of Japan, South Korea and European rivals in nuclear-hydrogen
  • Offers baseload, weather-independent hydrogen production unlike solar/wind electrolysis
  • Aligns with India's national green hydrogen mission targets

India has commissioned the world's first hydrogen production facility that harnesses nuclear technology, a landmark achievement that places the country at the vanguard of clean energy innovation. The facility, reported by Business Standard, uses nuclear energy to drive hydrogen production — a combination that has long been theorised as a cost-effective and low-carbon pathway but never before operationalised at this scale anywhere in the world.

Conventional green hydrogen is produced using electrolysis powered by renewable electricity, which remains expensive. Nuclear-powered hydrogen production offers a baseload, weather-independent energy source for the process, potentially making hydrogen cheaper and more reliably available. India's push into this space comes as the government has set ambitious targets for hydrogen as a fuel for industry, transport, and energy storage.

The development carries strategic significance beyond the technology itself. India is competing with Japan, South Korea, and several European nations to establish early leadership in hydrogen — a fuel widely expected to underpin the next phase of the global energy transition. Being first with a nuclear-hydrogen facility gives Indian research institutions and public-sector energy companies a valuable head start in refining the technology for commercial scale.

For everyday Indians, the longer-term implication is a potential reduction in dependence on imported fossil fuels. Domestically produced hydrogen, backed by India's growing nuclear capacity, could eventually feed into fertiliser manufacturing, steel production, and even public transport, sectors that are currently difficult to decarbonise. The facility's success will be closely watched by energy policymakers globally.
