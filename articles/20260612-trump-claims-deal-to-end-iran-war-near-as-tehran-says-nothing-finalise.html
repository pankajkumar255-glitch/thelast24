Trump claims deal to end Iran war near as Tehran says 'nothing' finalised

What happened

BBC and NDTV report that President Trump publicly claimed a deal to end the US-Iran confrontation was near, while Iranian officials stated that "nothing" had been finalised. NDTV further reports that Iran's conditions in a circulating draft deal include control over the Strait of Hormuz and the release of frozen Iranian assets. A separate NDTV report indicates that Israeli Prime Minister Netanyahu was reportedly kept in the dark about Trump's Iran plans and was left surprised by the developments. The contradictory public statements from both governments have heightened uncertainty over the status and substance of any potential agreement.

The context

The United States and Iran have had no formal diplomatic relations since 1980, following the Islamic Revolution and the hostage crisis. Tensions have periodically escalated over Iran's nuclear programme, with the landmark 2015 JCPOA (Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action) limiting Iran's nuclear activities in exchange for sanctions relief — a deal the US withdrew from in 2018 under Trump's first term. The Strait of Hormuz, a narrow waterway between Iran and Oman, is the world's most critical oil chokepoint, through which roughly 17-20 million barrels of oil pass daily. India imports a significant share of its crude oil from the Gulf region, making it highly sensitive to any disruption or stabilisation in the area.

Why it matters

A genuine US-Iran deal could ease sanctions, increase global oil supply, and bring down crude prices — a direct benefit for India which spends hundreds of billions of dollars annually on oil imports. Conversely, a breakdown in talks or an escalation could spike Brent crude prices and widen India's current account deficit. Indian refiners such as Indian Oil, BPCL, and HPCL, who have historically valued Iranian crude for its pricing, could benefit from any normalisation. The surprise to Israel also signals a potential realignment in Middle East geopolitics that India will need to navigate diplomatically.

What's next

Expect intensive back-channel diplomacy between Washington and Tehran in the coming days as both sides manage their public messaging. Watch for whether Iran formally responds to the draft deal terms and whether the Strait of Hormuz issue can be resolved as a sticking point. Global oil prices will be the immediate market indicator of how seriously traders assess the deal's prospects.
