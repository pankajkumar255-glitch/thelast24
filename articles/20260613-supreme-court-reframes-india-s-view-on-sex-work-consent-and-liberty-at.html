Supreme Court reframes India's view on sex work: consent and liberty at the centre

What happened

The Times of India reports that the Supreme Court has delivered a significant ruling on sex work that centres on consent and personal liberty, changing the legal and social lens through which India approaches the issue. The court's judgment recognises that adult sex workers who engage in their profession consensually have rights that must be protected, and that law enforcement cannot treat them as criminals solely on account of their occupation. The ruling is expected to have direct implications for how police conduct raids, how courts handle cases involving sex workers, and how welfare schemes are designed and delivered. The judgment adds to a growing body of Supreme Court jurisprudence that prioritises individual autonomy in matters of personal choice.

The context

Sex work has long occupied a legally ambiguous space in India — it is not explicitly illegal for an individual to engage in it, but activities surrounding it, such as running a brothel or soliciting in public, are criminalised under the Immoral Traffic (Prevention) Act, 1956. Sex workers in India have historically faced abuse, extortion, and arbitrary arrest by police, with little legal recourse. Organisations like the Durbar Mahila Samanwaya Committee in Kolkata have long campaigned for the rights and dignity of sex workers. Previous Supreme Court rulings and committees, including one led by Justice Verma, had moved toward recognising sex workers' rights. The intersections of gender, caste, poverty, and trafficking make this one of the most complex social issues before Indian courts.

Why it matters

For an estimated millions of sex workers across India, this ruling could mean greater legal protection from police harassment and arbitrary detention. It signals to law enforcement agencies that consent-based adult sex work cannot be treated as a crime, potentially reducing violence and exploitation faced by those in the profession. The ruling also sets a broader precedent for how Indian courts treat personal liberty and bodily autonomy in cases involving marginalised communities. Social welfare programmes targeting sex workers may need to be redesigned to focus on rights and support rather than rescue and rehabilitation imposed without consent.

What's next

Expect civil society groups and sex worker collectives to use the judgment to push for legislative reform of the Immoral Traffic (Prevention) Act. Law enforcement agencies will likely need revised guidelines on how to conduct operations involving sex workers. The government may face pressure to respond with a formal policy framework that aligns with the court's direction.
