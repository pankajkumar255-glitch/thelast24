Consensual pre-marital relationships not a blot on character, rules Supreme Court

What happened

The Hindu reports that the Supreme Court has explicitly stated that a consensual pre-marital physical relationship is not a mark on a person's character, issuing a clear rebuke to practices in Indian courts that have allowed such history to be weaponised against women. The ruling targets the long-standing use of a woman's sexual history as a tool to cast doubt on her credibility as a witness or complainant in criminal cases, particularly those involving sexual assault. NDTV also carried the ruling, indicating it has attracted widespread national attention. The judgment reinforces constitutional guarantees of equality and dignity by decoupling personal relationships from moral judgments about character. It is expected to have immediate impact on how judges and lawyers conduct themselves in sensitive criminal trials.

The context

Indian courts have had a troubled history with the 'two-finger test' and other practices that effectively put a rape survivor's character on trial. Section 155(4) of the Indian Evidence Act, which had allowed a woman's sexual history to be used to discredit her, was repealed in 2003, but the underlying attitudes persisted in courtrooms. The Criminal Law (Amendment) Act of 2013, enacted after the 2012 Delhi gang rape case, further strengthened protections for survivors, including restrictions on questions about past sexual history. Despite these legal safeguards, defence lawyers and some judges have continued to make or allow character-based observations. This Supreme Court ruling is a pointed restatement and reinforcement of those protections.

Why it matters

For survivors of sexual assault seeking justice in Indian courts, this ruling provides a stronger legal shield against character assassination in the courtroom. It sends a clear signal to trial court judges and lawyers that using consensual past relationships to discredit a woman is constitutionally impermissible. The ruling could lead to faster and fairer outcomes in sexual assault cases by narrowing the scope for irrelevant and harmful lines of questioning. It also contributes to a broader cultural shift in how Indian institutions view women's autonomy and personal choices.

What's next

Expect women's rights lawyers to cite this ruling in ongoing and future sexual assault trials to block character-based questioning. Bar councils and judicial training bodies are likely to be asked to incorporate this ruling into their training curricula for lawyers and judges.
