Supreme Court changes how India views sex work — consent and liberty at centre

What happened

The Times of India reports that the Supreme Court has delivered a ruling that changes the way India legally views sex work, with consent and liberty emerging as the central principles of the judgment. The court's position marks a clear departure from earlier frameworks that conflated voluntary sex work with exploitation or criminality. The ruling is expected to have far-reaching implications for how law enforcement agencies, courts, and welfare bodies interact with sex workers. The Times of India describes it as a defining moment in India's evolving jurisprudence on personal liberty and bodily autonomy.

The context

India's legal treatment of sex work has long been governed by the Immoral Traffic (Prevention) Act, 1956, which does not criminalise sex work itself but penalises activities around it such as running a brothel or soliciting in public. Despite this, sex workers have historically faced harassment, arbitrary detention, and denial of basic rights. The Supreme Court had previously, in 2022, directed that sex workers be treated with dignity and that police must not abuse them. Rights groups and legal advocates have for years argued that voluntary adult sex work should be distinguished from trafficking and coerced prostitution.

Why it matters

For an estimated millions of sex workers in India, this ruling could provide meaningful legal protection against arbitrary police action and social exclusion. It reinforces the constitutional right to dignity and personal liberty under Article 21 for a community that has long been denied these protections in practice. The emphasis on consent could also sharpen the legal distinction between voluntary sex work and trafficking, aiding prosecution of genuine exploitation. It signals that the court views adult autonomy as a foundational value.

What's next

Expect lower courts and police training bodies to be directed to align their practices with the Supreme Court's new framework. Rights organisations are likely to use this ruling as a precedent to push for broader legislative reforms to the Immoral Traffic (Prevention) Act.
