Anthropic Access Suspension Sparks India Self-Reliance Debate; Sridhar Vembu Says 'Globalisation Is Dead'

What happened

Deccan Herald reports that the suspension of Anthropic's most powerful AI models for non-US users has reignited India's self-reliance debate, with Zoho founder Sridhar Vembu publicly declaring 'Globalisation Is Dead' in the wake of the access row. Vembu's comments reflect a broader concern in Indian tech circles about the risks of depending on US-controlled AI platforms that are subject to unilateral policy changes. The incident has drawn attention from Indian technologists, policymakers, and entrepreneurs who see the episode as a warning sign about the fragility of India's AI ecosystem.

The context

Sridhar Vembu, founder and CEO of Zoho Corporation, is one of India's most prominent technology entrepreneurs and a consistent advocate for Indian software and AI self-sufficiency, having moved Zoho's headquarters to rural Tamil Nadu as a statement of intent about decentralised, indigenous development. India has made AI a national priority under its IndiaAI Mission, which includes funding for domestic compute infrastructure and model development. However, Indian startups and enterprises remain heavily reliant on APIs from US AI companies for frontier capabilities. The US has a long history of using export controls to restrict technology access for geopolitical reasons, and applying these to AI models is a significant escalation of that approach. The debate mirrors earlier discussions triggered by chip export restrictions that threatened India's semiconductor and data centre ambitions.

Why it matters

If powerful AI models can be switched off for Indian users by a US government directive with no notice, India's growing AI-dependent industries — from fintech and healthtech to enterprise software — face a systemic risk. The episode strengthens the argument for India to urgently scale its own large language model ecosystem rather than outsourcing that capability to US firms. It may also influence government policy on AI procurement and data sovereignty. For Indian consumers, the longer-term effect could be a more resilient domestic AI industry, but the short-term disruption is real and immediate.

What's next

Expect the Indian government and industry bodies to sharpen their focus on the IndiaAI Mission's domestic model development goals in response to this episode. Sridhar Vembu and others advocating for self-reliance are likely to find a more receptive audience among Indian policymakers and investors. Watch for fresh announcements of funding or partnerships aimed at accelerating homegrown frontier AI model development.
