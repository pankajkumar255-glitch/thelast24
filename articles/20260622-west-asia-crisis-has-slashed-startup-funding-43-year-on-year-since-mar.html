West Asia crisis has slashed startup funding 43% year-on-year since March

Key facts

  • Startup funding down 43% year-on-year since March
  • West Asia crisis cited as primary trigger for investor pullback
  • Decline follows already difficult 2022-23 funding winter
  • Early-stage and consumer internet startups most exposed

India's startup ecosystem has absorbed a sharp new blow, with funding falling 43% year-on-year since March — a decline that Business Standard's analysis links directly to the escalating West Asia crisis rattling global investor confidence. The drop reverses what had looked like tentative signs of recovery after the prolonged 2022-23 funding winter and puts pressure on hundreds of startups that had been bridging toward their next financing rounds.

The West Asia conflict has sent shockwaves through global risk markets, prompting large institutional limited partners — pension funds, sovereign wealth vehicles, and endowments that back venture capital funds — to reduce their exposure to higher-risk asset classes, including emerging-market technology investments. With LP commitments tightening, venture funds are deploying capital more cautiously, concentrating on existing portfolio companies rather than writing fresh cheques to new or early-stage Indian startups.

The impact is felt unevenly across the ecosystem. Consumer internet, edtech, and early-stage deep-tech ventures — segments that depend heavily on external venture money rather than revenues — face the sharpest squeeze. Growth-stage companies seeking large rounds are also finding fewer willing international co-investors, even where domestic capital is available. Several founders have reportedly extended runways by cutting costs rather than attempting raises in the current climate.

The broader concern is timing. Indian startups were anticipating 2025 as a year of normalisation after two difficult years. A 43% funding fall driven by an external geopolitical variable is harder to plan around than a domestic correction, and without a resolution in West Asia, investors see little near-term catalyst for sentiment to reverse.
