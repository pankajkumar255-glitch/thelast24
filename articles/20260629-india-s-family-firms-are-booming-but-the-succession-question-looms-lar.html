India's family firms are booming — but the succession question looms large

Key facts

  • India's family-owned businesses are currently on a strong growth path
  • Leadership succession is identified as a key unresolved challenge
  • Many of India's largest conglomerates are family-controlled
  • Poor succession planning poses governance and stability risks

India's family-owned businesses are on a strong growth trajectory, but beneath the boom lies a structural challenge that could define their long-term prospects: who leads them next. As The Times of India reports, the question of succession in India's family firms — many of which are among the country's most powerful economic entities — is becoming increasingly urgent as founders age and the business environment grows more complex.

Family-controlled groups dominate India's corporate landscape, from sprawling conglomerates in energy, retail, and telecommunications to mid-size manufacturers and regional champions. Many of these businesses were built over decades by visionary founders who combined entrepreneurial instinct with deep personal networks. Handing that mantle to the next generation — or to professional managers — requires not just a change of name at the top, but a fundamental shift in culture and governance.

Succession in Indian family firms has historically been handled informally, with decisions made within family councils rather than through transparent board processes. As these companies list on exchanges, attract institutional investors, and expand internationally, the pressure for more structured, merit-based leadership transitions has grown. Investors increasingly scrutinise whether the next generation has the capability and mandate to lead, or whether internal family disputes could destabilise operations.

The stakes are high: India's family businesses collectively employ a vast share of the formal workforce and contribute significantly to GDP. Getting succession right — or failing to — will reverberate well beyond the boardroom.
