As Modi Nears Nehru's Record, How India's Economy Has Transformed in 12 Years

What happened

Moneycontrol.com reports that as PM Narendra Modi approaches the record for India's longest-serving Prime Minister — a record currently held by Jawaharlal Nehru — the outlet has assessed how India's economy has changed across the 12 years of Modi's tenure. The analysis spans the period from 2014 to the present, covering multiple phases of economic policy including demonetisation, GST implementation, the COVID-19 shock, and the post-pandemic recovery. The piece positions Modi's economic legacy in comparison with the foundational economic architecture built under Nehru, including public sector enterprises and planned development. No specific data points are cited in the headline, but the framing suggests a comprehensive review of structural economic change.

The context

Jawaharlal Nehru served as India's Prime Minister from 1947 to 1964 — a span of nearly 17 years. Modi, who took office in May 2014, is approaching or has reached approximately 11-12 years in office, making the Nehru comparison increasingly relevant. The Modi era has been marked by flagship economic initiatives including Make in India, Digital India, the Pradhan Mantri Jan Dhan Yojana, demonetisation in 2016, the rollout of GST in 2017, and the post-COVID production-linked incentive (PLI) scheme. India's nominal GDP has grown substantially in dollar terms during this period, and the country has risen in global economic rankings. However, critics point to persistent unemployment, agrarian stress, and widening inequality as challenges that remain unresolved.

Why it matters

Assessing 12 years of economic transformation helps citizens, investors, and policymakers understand what structural changes are durable and what challenges remain unaddressed as India aims to become a developed economy by 2047. The comparison with Nehru's era also illuminates how India's economic philosophy has shifted from state-led planning to a mixed model with greater emphasis on private capital and digital delivery. For ordinary Indians, the scorecard on jobs, inflation, and welfare access is the most tangible measure of whether the transformation has been inclusive.

What's next

As India enters what could be the second half of the Modi government's current term, expect economic policy debates to intensify around employment generation, manufacturing competitiveness, and rural income growth — the areas where the 12-year record is most contested.
