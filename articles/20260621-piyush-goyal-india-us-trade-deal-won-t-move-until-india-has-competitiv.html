Piyush Goyal: India-US trade deal won't move until India has 'competitive advantage'

Key facts

  • Goyal: India-US trade deal won't be implemented without 'competitive advantage' for India
  • Talks cover tariffs, agriculture, digital trade and labour-intensive exports
  • India resisting US pressure to open sectors like agriculture and digital commerce
  • Statement signals New Delhi will not rush an early-harvest deal

India will not sign a trade deal with the United States until the agreement delivers a clear competitive advantage for Indian industries, Commerce Minister Piyush Goyal declared on Monday — a firm signal that New Delhi intends to negotiate on its own terms rather than yield to American timelines.

Goyal's remarks come at a sensitive moment in India-US trade relations, with both sides engaged in ongoing negotiations that cover a wide range of goods, tariffs and market-access conditions. Washington has been pressing India to open up in sectors including agriculture and digital trade, while New Delhi has sought concessions on labour-intensive exports such as textiles, gems and pharmaceuticals.

The minister's insistence on 'competitive advantage' as a prerequisite suggests that India is prepared to let talks drag on rather than accept an early-harvest deal that falls short of its core demands. This posture contrasts with the urgency some US counterparts have expressed for a swift agreement, particularly in the context of American tariff policy under the Trump administration.

For Indian businesses, the stance offers some reassurance that the government will not sacrifice domestic manufacturing interests at the altar of diplomatic goodwill. However, a prolonged stalemate also carries costs — particularly for exporters who stand to gain from lower US tariffs but remain in limbo. The coming weeks of negotiation will test whether Goyal's red lines hold or whether diplomatic pressure produces movement.
