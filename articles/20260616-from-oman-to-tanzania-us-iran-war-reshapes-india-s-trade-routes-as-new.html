From Oman to Tanzania: US-Iran war reshapes India's trade routes as new corridors surge

What happened

The Economic Times reports that the Iran war is redrawing India's trade map, with new corridors through Oman, Tanzania, Brazil, and Singapore gaining prominence. The Times of India similarly notes that Oman, Brazil, and Singapore are surging as India's trade routes are reshaped by the US-Iran conflict. Both outlets highlight the rerouting of merchandise flows away from traditional West Asian pathways.

The context

India relies heavily on West Asian sea lanes — particularly through the Strait of Hormuz and the Gulf of Oman — for oil imports, gold, and a wide range of goods. The US-Iran conflict has raised the risk and cost of these routes, echoing disruptions seen during the Red Sea crisis of 2024. India has been exploring alternative trade corridors, including the International North-South Transport Corridor (INSTC) and sea routes through East Africa.

Why it matters

Rerouting trade adds time and freight costs that eventually feed into prices for Indian consumers, particularly for oil and imported goods. However, emerging corridors through Oman and East Africa could also create new long-term trade partnerships that reduce India's dependence on any single route.

What's next

Expect Indian shipping companies, the government's logistics ministry, and major importers to fast-track alternative corridor agreements, particularly with Oman and East African nations.
