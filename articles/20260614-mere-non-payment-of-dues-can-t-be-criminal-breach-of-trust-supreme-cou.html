Mere non-payment of dues can't be criminal breach of trust: Supreme Court

What happened

Deccan Herald reports that the Supreme Court has held that the mere non-payment of dues cannot be treated as criminal breach of trust under Indian law. The court clarified the legal threshold required for such charges, distinguishing between a civil dispute over money owed and a criminal act involving dishonest intent. The ruling is likely to be cited in future cases where criminal breach of trust charges arise from commercial disputes.

The context

Criminal breach of trust under the Indian Penal Code (now the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita) has frequently been invoked in commercial disputes, often as a pressure tactic against debtors or business partners. Courts have repeatedly noted the misuse of criminal law to settle what are essentially civil matters, but clear judicial guidance has been lacking. This ruling provides a definitive standard for lower courts to follow.

Why it matters

The judgment offers protection to business owners, employers, and individuals against frivolous criminal complaints filed solely over non-payment, reducing harassment through misuse of the criminal justice system. It also helps unclog courts by discouraging the conversion of civil disputes into criminal cases.

What's next

Expect defence lawyers to cite this ruling widely in pending criminal breach of trust cases stemming from commercial or contractual disputes.
