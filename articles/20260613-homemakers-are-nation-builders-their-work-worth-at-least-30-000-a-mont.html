Homemakers are 'nation builders', their work worth at least ₹30,000 a month: Supreme Court

What happened

The Hindu reports that the Supreme Court has ruled that the work of homemakers deserves formal economic recognition, valuing it at a minimum of ₹30,000 per month. The court described homemakers as 'nation builders', underlining that domestic labour — cooking, childcare, elder care, household management — constitutes real, quantifiable economic contribution. The ruling is particularly significant in the context of accident compensation cases, where courts have historically assigned low or notional monetary values to the lives and work of housewives. By setting a minimum benchmark of ₹30,000 per month, the court provides lower courts with a concrete figure to apply in such cases. The judgment is seen as a step toward correcting a long-standing injustice in how the law values women's unpaid labour.

The context

Indian courts, including in Motor Accident Claims Tribunal cases, have for decades struggled to assign a monetary value to the work of homemakers because they earn no formal wages. Earlier Supreme Court rulings had made incremental steps toward recognising this work, but a concrete minimum figure had been absent. Studies by economists have consistently shown that if unpaid domestic labour were counted in GDP calculations, it would represent a significant share of national economic output. Women in India perform a disproportionate share of unpaid care and domestic work, a fact documented extensively by the National Sample Survey and international bodies. The issue has been a focus of feminist legal advocacy for years.

Why it matters

Families of homemakers who are killed or injured in road accidents or other incidents will now be entitled to significantly higher compensation from insurers and at-fault parties. The ₹30,000 per month benchmark will likely be applied by Motor Accident Claims Tribunals across the country, affecting thousands of pending and future cases. More broadly, the ruling sends a cultural signal that the state values women's domestic contributions, which could influence debates around social security, pension rights, and welfare schemes for homemakers. It also puts pressure on insurance companies to revise how they price and calculate payouts in relevant policies.

What's next

Expect a wave of pending compensation cases to be revisited in light of this benchmark, with claimants' lawyers citing the ruling to demand higher awards. Legal experts and women's rights groups are likely to push for the ₹30,000 figure to be revised upward periodically in line with inflation and cost-of-living changes.
