Homemakers are 'nation builders', their work worth at least ₹30,000 a month: Supreme Court

What happened

The Hindu reports that the Supreme Court made the observation valuing homemakers' work at a minimum of ₹30,000 per month, describing them as 'nation builders.' The court's remarks came in the context of a case where the economic contribution of a homemaker needed to be quantified. The bench underscored that domestic work — cooking, childcare, household management — carries real monetary value that courts must recognise. This is not the first time the Supreme Court has made such an observation, but the specific ₹30,000 figure and the 'nation builders' framing give this ruling particular weight.

The context

Indian courts have historically struggled to assign monetary value to unpaid domestic labour in cases involving compensation for death or disability. Motor accident tribunals and insurance companies have often used outdated or arbitrarily low figures when calculating the economic loss caused by the death or incapacitation of a homemaker. The Supreme Court has previously directed that notional income be attributed to homemakers for compensation purposes, but a uniform standard has remained elusive. Women constitute a vast majority of India's homemakers, and their economic contribution to the household remains almost entirely invisible in official GDP calculations.

Why it matters

This ruling sets a judicial benchmark of ₹30,000 per month for homemakers' labour, which could substantially raise payouts in motor accident and insurance cases involving homemakers. It gives families — particularly those who lose a homemaker to accident or illness — a stronger basis to claim fair compensation. More broadly, it advances a legal recognition that domestic labour has economic value, which could eventually influence social security, insurance, and policy frameworks. The ruling also carries symbolic weight in a country where women's unpaid work is routinely overlooked.

What's next

Expect motor accident tribunals and insurance companies to begin referencing the ₹30,000 figure in compensation calculations going forward. Legal advocates may use this ruling to push for a more standardised, inflation-linked formula for valuing homemakers' work in all future cases.
