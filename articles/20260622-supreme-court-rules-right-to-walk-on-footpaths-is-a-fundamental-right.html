Supreme Court Rules Right to Walk on Footpaths Is a Fundamental Right

Key facts

  • Supreme Court declares right to walk on footpaths a fundamental right
  • Targets encroachment by vendors, vehicles, and illegal structures
  • Municipal bodies now legally obligated to maintain clear footpaths
  • Has implications for pedestrian safety across all Indian cities

The Supreme Court of India has ruled that the right to walk on footpaths constitutes a fundamental right, in a judgment that holds sweeping implications for urban governance and pedestrian safety across the country. The court's ruling, reported by Business Standard, frames unobstructed pedestrian access not as a civic amenity but as a constitutionally protected entitlement.

The judgment directly targets the near-universal problem in Indian cities where footpaths are routinely encroached upon by street vendors, illegally parked two-wheelers and cars, construction material, and utility poles — leaving pedestrians with no option but to walk on busy roads. This is a leading cause of pedestrian fatalities in Indian cities, which record some of the highest road accident death tolls globally.

By categorising footpath access as a fundamental right, the Supreme Court creates a powerful legal instrument for citizens to hold municipal corporations and state governments accountable. Civic bodies can now face contempt proceedings or writ petitions if they fail to ensure footpaths remain clear and accessible.

Urban planners and pedestrian rights advocates have long argued that Indian city planning systematically prioritises vehicle movement over the safety of those on foot. This ruling is expected to catalyse fresh litigation and policy action, particularly in metros like Delhi, Mumbai, Bengaluru, and Chennai, where pavement encroachment is chronic and enforcement has historically been negligible.
