Will a nation grow, stagnate, decline? Technology will tell

Key facts

  • Technology adoption now seen as primary driver of national growth or decline
  • India's digital infrastructure (UPI, Aadhaar) cited as a foundation to build on
  • Next five years framed as decisive for India's technology trajectory

Technology has become the master variable of national destiny — more predictive of a country's future than its demographics, natural endowments, or even its governance structures. That is the central argument of a sweeping analysis published by The Indian Express, which examines how the divergence between technology-forward nations and laggards is accelerating in ways that may soon be irreversible.

The piece points to the compounding nature of technological advantage: countries that invest early in digital infrastructure, AI capability, and STEM education generate productivity gains that allow them to invest further, creating a virtuous cycle. Conversely, nations that delay or deprioritise technology adoption find the catch-up task growing harder with each passing year, as the frontier moves faster than their capacity to close the gap.

For India, the analysis arrives at a moment of genuine choice. The country has demonstrated its ability to build large-scale digital public infrastructure — Aadhaar, UPI, and the ONDC network are globally cited examples — but translating that foundation into broad-based productivity improvement across agriculture, manufacturing, and services remains a work in progress. The next five years are widely seen as decisive for whether India locks in a high-growth technology trajectory or settles into a middle-income equilibrium.

The broader message — that technology is no longer a sector but a civilisational operating system — has implications for how India allocates its education budgets, structures its trade policy, and engages with global technology governance. Nations that grasp this shift and act on it, the analysis suggests, will grow. Those that do not, will not.
