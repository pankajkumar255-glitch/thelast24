FIFA World Cup 2026: Fans Rage as US Travel Bans and Visa Rules Lock Out Supporters

What happened

BBC reports that international fans are expressing fury over US travel bans and visa restrictions ahead of the FIFA World Cup 2026, which is to be held in the United States, Canada and Mexico. A separate BBC report notes that Iran specifically complained that its staff were blocked from entering the US even after Iranian players were granted World Cup visas, suggesting an inconsistent and politically charged application of entry rules. Supporters from multiple affected nations have coined the phrase 'a World Cup for them, not us' to capture their sense of exclusion from a global sporting event being held on US soil.

The context

The FIFA World Cup 2026 is set to be the largest in history, expanded to 48 teams and spread across 16 host cities in three countries. The United States has pursued increasingly restrictive immigration and entry policies under the Trump administration, including travel bans targeting citizens of several nations. Hosting a global sporting event while maintaining such restrictions creates an inherent tension that FIFA and US authorities have not yet publicly resolved. Iran and the US have no formal diplomatic relations, adding a particularly sharp political dimension to the visa dispute.

Why it matters

India has a large and growing football fan base, and Indian passport holders may also face heightened scrutiny when applying for US visas to attend the tournament. More broadly, the episode illustrates how US immigration policy under the Trump administration is creating practical barriers to international travel, which affects Indian students, tourists, professionals and sports fans alike. It also raises questions about whether the US is a reliable host for future global events.

What's next

Expect FIFA to face mounting pressure to seek guarantees from the US government on visa access for fans and team officials from all 48 qualifying nations. Watch for whether any teams threaten to withdraw or whether the US offers diplomatic assurances ahead of the June 2026 tournament.
