Indian-American Sriram Krishnan Quits as White House AI Adviser After 18 Months

What happened

NDTV reports that Sriram Krishnan has resigned from his position as Senior Policy Adviser for Artificial Intelligence at the White House Office of Science and Technology Policy, after serving for approximately 18 months. Krishnan, a former venture capitalist and technology executive with prior stints at Microsoft, Twitter, and Andreessen Horowitz, was appointed to the role in early 2025. His tenure covered pivotal AI policy decisions including the revision of AI chip export rules and the development of a US national AI strategy. No reason for his departure has been specified in the available report.

The context

Krishnan was appointed to the White House AI advisory role at the start of the second Trump administration and was widely seen as a moderate, pro-immigration voice within an administration that has otherwise taken a harder line on foreign talent. His Indian origin made him a symbolic figure for the Indian-American technology community and for India-US technology diplomacy. US AI policy under his watch included significant decisions on semiconductor export controls — rules that directly determine which countries and companies can access Nvidia's most advanced AI chips. The role intersects with India's own AI ambitions, as Indian firms and researchers depend heavily on access to US-made AI hardware and models.

Why it matters

Krishnan's exit could shift the balance of opinion within White House AI policymaking, potentially toward more restrictive stances on AI chip exports or immigration of skilled tech workers — both of which have direct consequences for India. Indian companies and startups that rely on US AI platforms and hardware should watch who replaces him and what policy signals emerge. For the Indian-American diaspora in Silicon Valley, his departure closes a direct line of community representation in executive-branch AI decisions. More broadly, leadership transitions in AI policy roles carry outsized consequence given how rapidly the regulatory landscape is moving.

What's next

Expect the White House to name a successor within weeks, and watch whether the new appointee maintains Krishnan's relatively open approach to AI talent and technology sharing. Indian policymakers and industry bodies will likely monitor any policy shifts that follow, particularly on semiconductor export rules.
