Indian-American Sriram Krishnan Quits as White House AI Adviser After 18 Months

What happened

NDTV reports that Sriram Krishnan has resigned as the White House's senior policy adviser on artificial intelligence after serving in the role for 18 months. Krishnan, an Indian-American technologist with a background at companies including Microsoft and Andreessen Horowitz, was appointed to advise the administration on AI strategy, regulation, and industry engagement. NDTV does not report a stated reason for his departure or name an immediate replacement. The exit comes at a time when the US government is actively debating AI regulation, export controls on chips, and international AI governance frameworks, making the policy advisory role particularly consequential.

The context

Sriram Krishnan joined the White House AI policy office as part of the US government's effort to bring Silicon Valley expertise into federal AI governance. He previously held senior roles at Twitter, Microsoft, and was a general partner at Andreessen Horowitz, one of Silicon Valley's most influential venture capital firms. The US has been navigating complex questions around AI safety standards, semiconductor export controls affecting India and other countries, and its competitive posture relative to China. Krishnan's Indian heritage made him a figure of particular interest to India's tech community, and he has spoken publicly about the importance of global AI collaboration. White House AI advisory positions have seen considerable turnover as administrations balance industry input with regulatory priorities.

Why it matters

US AI policy directly affects Indian technology companies, particularly those involved in semiconductor supply chains, cloud services, and AI software exports. A change in the White House AI advisory team could shift emphasis in regulatory priorities in ways that open or close opportunities for Indian firms. The departure also signals potential instability in US AI policy continuity at a moment when international standards and export rules are being actively written. For the Indian diaspora in tech, Krishnan represented significant visibility at the highest levels of US policy-making.

What's next

Expect the White House to announce a successor appointment, whose background and priorities will be closely watched by both Silicon Valley and international governments including India. Watch whether Krishnan's exit prompts commentary about the revolving door between the tech industry and US government AI roles.
