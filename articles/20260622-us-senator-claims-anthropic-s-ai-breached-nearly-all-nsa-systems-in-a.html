US Senator claims Anthropic's AI breached nearly all NSA systems in a hours in a simulation

Key facts

  • Anthropic's AI allegedly breached nearly all NSA systems in hours
  • Claim made by a US Senator in an official context
  • Exercise described as a simulation or controlled demonstration
  • Anthropic known for Claude AI models and AI-safety focus

A sitting US Senator has claimed that Anthropic's AI model successfully breached nearly all systems belonging to the National Security Agency — one of the world's most secure intelligence organisations — in a matter of hours during what appears to have been a simulated or controlled exercise. The claim, reported by NDTV Profit, has sent fresh shockwaves through the already fraught debate over AI and national security.

The precise nature of the exercise — whether a formal red-team drill commissioned by the NSA, an internal Anthropic capability test, or a demonstration before lawmakers — has not been fully detailed in available reports. What is clear is that the Senator's account was offered in a legislative or security-briefing context, lending it at least some institutional weight. Anthropic, known for its Claude family of AI models and its stated focus on AI safety, has not publicly commented on the specific claim.

The allegation follows the Five Eyes intelligence alliance's rare public warning, issued the same day, that AI models capable of destabilising governments and businesses are only months away. Together, the two disclosures paint a sharply escalating picture: frontier AI is not merely a future national-security risk, but potentially a present one, and even the most hardened institutions may be more exposed than previously acknowledged.

For the broader public and policymakers alike, the claim underscores a troubling asymmetry — AI-enabled attackers can probe thousands of system vulnerabilities in parallel, at machine speed, while defenders must still prioritise and patch one weakness at a time. The episode is likely to accelerate calls in the US Congress for binding AI security regulations, and will resonate in capital cities far beyond Washington.
