India has nearly $700 billion in forex reserves — so why is foreign capital still a concern?

What happened

India Today reports that despite India holding nearly $700 billion in foreign exchange reserves, concerns remain about the quality and stability of foreign capital flows underpinning those reserves. The story examines the distinction between stable FDI and more volatile FPI inflows, and what a sudden reversal could mean for the rupee and the broader economy.

The context

India's forex reserves have grown significantly over the past decade, giving the RBI a substantial buffer to defend the rupee. However, a meaningful portion of the reserve build-up has come from foreign portfolio flows into equity and debt markets, which can reverse rapidly during global risk-off episodes, as seen during the 2013 taper tantrum and the 2022 FPI selloff.

Why it matters

Rupee stability directly affects the price of imports including oil, electronics, and fertilisers; a sharp depreciation driven by capital outflows would push up costs across the economy, increasing inflation and squeezing household budgets for hundreds of millions of Indians.

What's next

Expect the RBI to continue its policy of building FDI-backed reserves while managing portfolio flow volatility, with the central bank's currency intervention strategy likely to be a key watch point in the months ahead.
