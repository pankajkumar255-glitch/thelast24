Pakistan Insists Indus Waters Treaty Is 'Valid, Binding and Operative' as Tensions Simmer

Key facts

  • Pakistan Deputy PM declares treaty 'valid, binding and operative'
  • Bilawal Bhutto warns of 'nuclear armageddon' scenario
  • India suspended treaty following the Pahalgam terror attack
  • Treaty originally signed in 1960 under World Bank mediation

Pakistan's Deputy Prime Minister has declared the Indus Waters Treaty 'valid, binding and operative', in a direct challenge to India's decision to suspend the 1960 water-sharing agreement following the Pahalgam terror attack and the military escalation that followed. The statement signals Islamabad's intent to contest any unilateral Indian move to set aside the World Bank-brokered treaty, which has survived multiple wars between the two countries.

The rhetoric from Pakistan's leadership has grown markedly more alarming in recent days. Bilawal Bhutto has raised the possibility of a 'nuclear armageddon' scenario, while other officials have told international partners that abandoning the treaty would cause the 'world order to collapse' — appeals clearly designed to draw in global mediators and place pressure on New Delhi before any irreversible steps are taken.

India's position is that Pakistan's sponsorship of cross-border terrorism makes the continued operation of the treaty untenable, and that water-sharing commitments cannot be divorced from broader security obligations. New Delhi has framed the suspension as a legitimate pressure point rather than a permanent abrogation, but the signal to Islamabad has been unambiguous.

The treaty, signed in 1960 after years of World Bank-mediated negotiations, divides the six rivers of the Indus basin between the two countries and has historically been regarded as one of the most durable international water-sharing agreements in the world. Its fate is now deeply entangled with the broader state of India-Pakistan relations, and any prolonged suspension would have severe consequences for Pakistani agriculture, which depends on Indus system waters for irrigation across its most fertile regions.
