Anthropic Access Row Sparks India Self-Reliance Debate; Sridhar Vembu Says 'Globalisation Is Dead'

What happened

Deccan Herald reports that the Anthropic access controversy has reignited the self-reliance debate in India's technology sector, with Zoho founder Sridhar Vembu publicly declaring 'Globalisation Is Dead' in response to the US export control directive that cut off access to Anthropic's Fable 5 and Mythos 5 models. Vembu's remarks reflect a broader sentiment among Indian technologists who have long advocated for domestic AI capabilities.

The context

Sridhar Vembu is one of India's most prominent technology entrepreneurs and a vocal advocate of building indigenous tech capacity, having moved Zoho's operations to rural Tamil Nadu. His views carry significant weight in Indian tech and policy circles. India has been working on its own AI mission and funding domestic large language model research, but frontier models comparable to those from Anthropic or OpenAI remain distant goals.

Why it matters

If Indian businesses and developers cannot rely on continued access to leading foreign AI models, the economic case for investing in homegrown AI infrastructure becomes far more urgent, potentially redirecting significant capital and policy attention. For everyday Indians, the quality and availability of AI-powered services — from healthcare to education to fintech — could ultimately depend on whether India builds its own AI stack.

What's next

Expect Vembu's remarks to fuel louder calls in Parliament and industry bodies for accelerated funding and policy support for India's domestic AI model development programmes.
