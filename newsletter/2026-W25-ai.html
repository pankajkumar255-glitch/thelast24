This week in Artificial Intelligence

The 6 stories that mattered, 21 Jun–28 Jun.

18:32 IST

Five Eyes warns AI models capable of toppling governments and businesses are months away

A rare joint statement from the Five Eyes intelligence alliance — comprising the US, UK, Canada, Australia, and New Zealand — has warned that AI models powerful enough to destabilise governments and major businesses could be available within months. The statement marks an unusually public intervention by the world's most powerful intelligence-sharing bloc on the risks of frontier AI. It signals that security agencies are treating advanced AI as an imminent national-security threat rather than a distant concern.

→ When the world's top five intelligence agencies jointly sound the alarm on a technology's danger to governments and economies, Indian policymakers and businesses have strong reason to urgently audit their own AI readiness and cybersecurity frameworks.

20:51 IST

Meta taps CRED founder Kunal Shah as WhatsApp's new chief, invests $900M in his startup

Meta has appointed Kunal Shah, the Indian entrepreneur who founded fintech startup CRED, as the new head of WhatsApp, marking a significant leadership change at the world's most widely used messaging platform. Alongside the appointment, Meta is investing $900 million in CRED, a substantial bet on the Indian fintech ecosystem. Shah's elevation to lead WhatsApp underscores Meta's deepening engagement with India, its largest market by user numbers. The dual move — a C-suite appointment and a near-billion-dollar investment — signals a strategic pivot in how the company is approaching its messaging future.

→ An Indian founder now runs WhatsApp — the app used by hundreds of millions of Indians daily — while Meta's $900M investment in CRED is one of the largest bets ever placed on an Indian startup.

16:47 IST

A Viral 'Doomsday Scenario' Is Trying to Shock Europe Into Taking AI Seriously

A viral doomsday scenario is circulating in European policy circles, deliberately designed to jolt European governments and institutions out of what critics call AI complacency. The scenario reportedly paints a stark picture of what could happen if Europe continues to lag behind the United States and China in AI investment, regulation implementation, and indigenous model development. It reflects growing alarm among some European AI researchers and policymakers that the continent is sleepwalking into irrelevance on the defining technology of the era.

→ Europe's approach to AI — cautious regulation versus aggressive investment — is shaping global AI governance norms that will directly affect how Indian companies access, export to, and comply with AI standards in one of India's largest trading blocs.

15:34 IST

Google DeepMind Loses Nobel Prize Winner John Jumper to Anthropic

John Jumper, the Nobel Prize-winning scientist celebrated for his work on AlphaFold's protein-structure predictions, has left Google DeepMind to join Anthropic. The move is a significant talent coup for Anthropic and a notable blow to DeepMind, which has positioned Jumper's research as a crown jewel of its scientific credibility. Jumper shared the 2024 Nobel Prize in Chemistry alongside DeepMind CEO Demis Hassabis for their work on AlphaFold.

→ When a Nobel laureate switches AI labs, it signals which frontier research organisations are winning the talent war — and that race will shape what breakthroughs reach ordinary people first.

10:36 IST

Trump Says He No Longer Views Anthropic as a National Security Threat

US President Donald Trump has reportedly reversed his earlier characterisation of Anthropic, stating he no longer considers the AI company a national security threat. The shift marks a notable change in the administration's posture toward the AI safety-focused lab, which had been viewed with suspicion due to its ties to foreign investment and its founding team's background. The development could ease regulatory and political pressure on Anthropic as it scales its operations.

→ How Washington classifies AI companies shapes which ones can raise money, hire globally, and expand freely — and that directly affects which AI tools reach Indian businesses and consumers.

10:41 IST

Tech Workers Who Maxed Out AI Use Are Now Trying to Minimise It

A growing number of tech workers who once pushed their AI tool usage to the limit are now actively pulling back, raising new questions about productivity, dependency, and the real-world value of AI in daily professional life. The trend, reported by The Indian Express, reflects mounting concerns among knowledge workers about skill atrophy, over-reliance, and dissatisfaction with AI-generated outputs. The backlash is emerging precisely among the cohort expected to champion AI adoption.

→ If even the most enthusiastic early-adopting tech workers are dialling back AI use, it raises a direct question for Indian professionals racing to upskill with these tools: are they building capability or ceding it?
